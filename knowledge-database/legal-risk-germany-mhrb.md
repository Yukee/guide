# Legal Risk: Ordering Mimosa Hostilis Root Bark to Germany

This entry analyzes the legal risk of a specific scenario: a German cluster headache patient orders Mimosa hostilis root bark (MHRB) from a Netherlands-based online shop, with the stated purpose of dyeing clothes. This scenario is relevant because MHRB is widely available from Dutch smartshops and is one of the most accessible sources of DMT for self-treatment. The bottom line: ordering MHRB into Germany carries real criminal risk, but enforcement outcomes vary dramatically depending on quantity, circumstances, and the buyer's ability to credibly demonstrate non-drug intent. Multiple German courts have acquitted defendants who claimed dyeing purposes — but those acquittals came only after months-long criminal investigations.

For the international and US-focused legal context, see [legal-status-and-detection.md](legal-status-and-detection.md).

## German Legal Framework

### DMT under the BtMG

DMT (dimethyltryptamine) is listed in Anlage I (Schedule I) of the Betäubungsmittelgesetz (BtMG — Narcotic Drugs Act) [1][2]. Anlage I substances are classified as non-traffickable: they cannot be legally possessed, acquired, imported, manufactured, or traded without government authorization, which is granted only for scientific or medical purposes [1].

Under § 29 Abs.1 BtMG, unauthorized handling of Anlage I substances is punishable by fines or imprisonment up to 5 years [3]. When the quantity involved exceeds the "nicht geringe Menge" (not small amount) threshold, penalties escalate to 1–15 years under § 29a BtMG [3][4].

### MHRB's legal status

Mimosa hostilis (Mimosa tenuiflora) root bark is not explicitly listed by name in the BtMG [5]. It is treated as a controlled substance because it contains DMT. However, there is an important legal nuance: German courts have recognized that plants containing controlled substances are only criminal to possess if they are intended for intoxication ("Missbrauch zu Rauschzwecken") [6][7]. When the plant material is possessed for a legitimate purpose — such as use as a textile dye — some courts have held that no criminal offense exists (see Court Cases below).

This creates a legal gray area. Law enforcement and some legal commentators take the position that MHRB is simply illegal because it contains DMT, regardless of stated purpose [5][8]. Other courts and defense attorneys have successfully argued that intent matters and that MHRB marketed as a dye product falls outside the BtMG [6][7][9].

### The "nicht geringe Menge" threshold

The Landgericht Frankenthal (Regional Court) established in December 2012 that the "nicht geringe Menge" threshold for DMT is **3.6 grams of DMT base**, calculated as 120 consumption units at 30 mg each (case 5127 Js 10022/09) [4][10]. The Landgericht Würzburg independently confirmed this same threshold in October 2022 (case 8 KLs 822 Js 14426/19), after its own expert consultation [19]. Neither decision has been reviewed by the BGH (Federal Court of Justice), and the threshold's correctness has been questioned by some legal commentators [10].

### DMT concentration in MHRB: what courts and forensic labs have found

The DMT content of MHRB varies far more than commonly assumed. Scientific literature typically cites 0.57–1.0% [19], but **forensic analyses of seized material have found significantly higher concentrations**:

| Source | DMT content | Context |
|--------|------------|---------|
| Scientific literature | 0.31–1.0% | Schultes 1977, Fasanello & Placke 2007 [11][20] |
| LG Frankenthal (2012) | ~1.51% | Lab analysis of seized 1.6 kg (24.2g DMT) [4] |
| LKA Hamburg (2022) | **2.44%** | NMR quantitation of seized 572g, published in forensic journal [19] |
| University of Saarland (2018) | **3.01%** | Forensic analysis of ~11.5 kg from raided online shop; cited in AG Rostock [6][21] |
| LKA Hamburg general statement | up to 2.5–3.0% | "From forensic investigations it is known that DMT contents up to approximately 2.5–3.0% can also be present" [19] |

The Hamburg State Criminal Police Office (LKA) attributes this variation to growth conditions, processing, storage conditions, and packaging [19].

This means the quantity of MHRB needed to reach the 3.6g threshold depends heavily on the batch:

| MHRB quantity | At 1% DMT | At 2% DMT | At 3% DMT |
|---------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| 100g | 1.0g | 2.0g | 3.0g |
| 120g | 1.2g | 2.4g | **3.6g** — at threshold |
| 180g | 1.8g | **3.6g** — at threshold | 5.4g |
| 250g | 2.5g | 5.0g | 7.5g |
| 360g | **3.6g** — at threshold | 7.2g | 10.8g |
| 500g | 5.0g | 10.0g | 15.0g |

Exceeding the threshold shifts the offense from § 29 (up to 5 years) to § 29a (1–15 years) and may prompt authorities to conduct a house search [8].

### Court practice on DMT analysis

German courts are inconsistent about whether they order independent laboratory analysis of seized MHRB:

- **LG Frankenthal (2012)**: Full lab analysis performed — found ~1.51% DMT [4]
- **AG Rostock (2021)**: Lab analysis performed on the *shop operator's* stock (3.01%), but not on the defendant's specific batch. The court applied "in dubio pro reo" (benefit of the doubt) and assumed the defendant's 1,000g contained DMT in the **lowest range of 0.1–0.3%** — far below the threshold [6][21]
- **AG Geilenkirchen (2020)**: **No analysis performed at all** — the substance was never tested for DMT content [7]
- **LKA Hamburg case (2022)**: Full NMR quantitation — found 2.44% [19]

Defense attorneys recommend that defendants **always insist on individual analysis** of the seized material, since concentrations vary significantly and the assumed "standard" of ~1% may be inaccurate in either direction [10][19].

## Netherlands Legal Status

### The purchase is legal in the Netherlands

MHRB (the raw plant bark) is legal to sell and purchase in the Netherlands [12]. Multiple Dutch smartshops openly sell it online as an ethnobotanical product, including Herbal Spirit, NL Naturals, Gaiana, and others. The plant itself and its unprocessed parts (bark, leaves, flowers) are not controlled under Dutch law [12].

### DMT is controlled

DMT is classified as a List I substance (hard drug) under the Dutch Opiumwet (Opium Act) [13]. The key distinction: the raw plant material is legal; the extracted alkaloid and preparations containing DMT are not. The Dutch Supreme Court ruled in 2018 that ayahuasca (a DMT-containing brew prepared from plant material) falls under the Opiumwet prohibition [13].

### Cross-border implication

Purchasing MHRB from a Dutch smartshop is a legal transaction in the Netherlands. The criminal exposure under German law begins when the package crosses into German territory or arrives at the recipient's address in Germany.

## How Investigations Are Initiated

Based on documented cases, **the primary enforcement mechanism is not customs x-raying individual parcels — it is seizing vendor customer lists**. Both pathways are described below.

### Vendor raids and customer list seizures

This is the documented trigger for the majority of known investigations. Two patterns have been identified:

**German-based sellers:** An eBay shop based in Saarbrücken that sold MHRB was raided by police. Customer purchase data was seized, and buyers across Germany were subsequently contacted by investigators [5][22][23]. According to attorney sources and forum reports, "thousands of people have been surprised by summons" based on these records [5][8]. The AG Rostock defendant (1,000g, acquitted) purchased from this eBay shop [6][21]. The forensic analysis of ~11.5 kg of root bark seized from this shop (University of Saarland, 2018) found an average DMT content of 3.01% [6][21].

**Dutch-based sellers:** At least one Dutch online shop selling MHRB was shut down ("hochgenommen"), and customer data was obtained by German authorities [24]. A forum account describes ordering 50g from this Dutch shop, then receiving an investigation notice approximately three years later [24]. The person had also ordered Harmala seeds from the same vendor. Two additional JustAnswer.de legal consultation threads describe individuals who ordered MHRB from Dutch online shops and subsequently faced police contact, though the threads are behind paywalls and it is unclear whether these were triggered by vendor customer list seizures or customs interceptions [34][35].

Beyond these reports, **no additional documented cases of foreign MHRB vendor customer list seizures reaching German authorities have been identified** — despite extensive searching of German and English-language forums (DMT-Nexus, Entheobotanik.net, Shroomery, Bluelight, Reddit), attorney publications, court records, and law enforcement press releases. Europol and Eurojust have published no operations specifically targeting MHRB or ethnobotanical plant material vendors. This is consistent with DMT being an enforcement afterthought: the BKA does not track it as a separate substance category, and the 2023 PKS recorded only 580 consumption-related LSD offenses nationwide — DMT would be a fraction of even that [25].

**Why foreign vendor raids appear rare:** Since MHRB is legal to sell in the Netherlands (and several other EU countries), Dutch authorities have no independent reason to raid an MHRB vendor. A raid would require a German request through the **European Investigation Order (EIO)** — the EU mutual legal assistance mechanism that allows a German prosecutor to order a Dutch authority to gather evidence, including customer data from a vendor. This is resource-intensive for what German prosecutors treat as a low-priority offense. The Entheobotanik.net forum thread on MHRB investigations includes a discussion of whether ordering from Dutch or Spanish shops would make it harder for German authorities to access order data; no participant could cite a specific cross-border vendor customer list case beyond the suchtmittel.de report [22][23]. However, the fact that at least one Dutch shop's customer data *did* reach German investigators demonstrates that the mechanism works — the question is how often it is used, not whether it can be used.

**Parallel pattern — German precedent for vendor-to-customer investigations:** The same customer-list-seizure pattern has been documented for other drug-adjacent products in Germany: the CBD shop Cannaheld was raided in September 2021, customer and billing data were seized, and customers were notified only in October 2022, followed by mass investigation proceedings. Growshops in Bremen and Hannover (including Grasshopper, Udopea, and Euphoria) were raided starting in 2014, and customer data from those shops led to house searches of customers that continued for years. These are all German-based vendors, but they confirm that the legal mechanism — seizing vendor records and systematically investigating buyers — is standard German prosecutorial practice, not specific to MHRB.

The delay between purchase and investigation can be **years** — in documented cases, 3 years (Dutch shop) and 3 years (Saarbrücken eBay shop, where a buyer who purchased 200g in March 2017 received a police visit in March 2020) [22][24].

### Customs interception

Although intra-EU parcels are not subject to mandatory customs declarations under the single market, German postal distribution centers (Briefzentren) conduct random and targeted inspections of parcels [14]. Plant powder can trigger further inspection, as customs officers may flag it as potentially drug-related [14].

The LG Frankenthal case (2012) is the only documented case clearly triggered by customs interception: a package from a Netherlands-based retailer was seized in transit [4][15]. There is no publicly available data on interception rates for intra-EU parcels.

### Scale of enforcement

According to one German criminal defense attorney's analysis, over 1,000 criminal investigations were ongoing nationwide in Germany related to the acquisition of Mimosa hostilis as of approximately 2022 [5]. Forum users have questioned the accuracy of this figure [22], but the scale of vendor-list-based investigations (affecting "thousands" of buyers from a single eBay shop) is consistent with it.

## What Happens After Detection

Whether triggered by customs interception or a vendor customer list, the typical sequence is:

1. **Seizure (Beschlagnahme)**: The package is confiscated and its contents may be sent for laboratory analysis.
2. **Criminal investigation opened**: Proceedings are initiated against the named recipient under § 29 BtMG.
3. **Police summons (Vorladung)**: The suspect receives a written invitation to provide a statement. Defense attorneys universally advise declining this invitation and instead hiring counsel who will request access to the case file (Akteneinsicht) [5][8][9].
4. **Possible house search (Hausdurchsuchung)**: If the quantity suggests a "nicht geringe Menge" or if authorities suspect distribution, a search warrant (Durchsuchungsbeschluss) may be obtained and executed [5][8].
5. **Resolution**: The case concludes in one of several ways: dismissal by the prosecution under § 170(2) StPO (insufficient evidence), conditional dismissal under § 153a StPO (minor offense, possibly with a fine), penalty order (Strafbefehl), or trial.

**Timeline**: Proceedings typically take 4–8 months from interception to resolution [8].

**Important**: The recipient's name on a package does not definitively prove they placed the order. Defense attorneys have successfully argued that someone else may have used the defendant's name and address [9][16].

## Court Cases and Outcomes

### Conviction: LG Frankenthal (December 2012)

The Landgericht Frankenthal convicted a defendant who ordered 1.6 kg of MHRB (containing approximately 24.2g DMT) from a Netherlands-based retailer. This case established the 3.6g DMT threshold for "nicht geringe Menge." The defendant was convicted of attempted unauthorized possession of narcotics in a not small amount [4][10].

### Acquittal: AG Rostock (May 10, 2021)

Case 29 Ls 42/20. The defendant purchased 1,000g of MHRB via eBay for EUR 145.98 in May 2017, claiming it was for textile dyeing (batik-style T-shirts for festivals). The eBay shop — later raided in Saarbrücken — offered the product exclusively as a dye. A University of Saarland forensic report (October 2018) found the shop's stock contained an average of 3.01% DMT, which would put the defendant's 1,000g at 30.1g DMT — 8.4 times the threshold. However, the court applied "in dubio pro reo" and assumed the defendant's specific batch contained DMT in the lowest range (0.1–0.3%), noting that plant product concentrations can diverge significantly. The Amtsgericht Rostock acquitted him, finding that he lacked knowledge of the DMT content and had no reason to suspect illegal components [6][21].

### Acquittal: AG Geilenkirchen (August 18, 2020)

A 31-year-old defendant purchased 500g of MHRB for EUR 87.90. He stated he intended to dye a T-shirt in 1980s style for a costume party, dissolving the powder in warm bathwater overnight, and discarded the remainder after unsatisfactory results. The substance was never analyzed for DMT content. Judge Thomas Schönig held that acquiring MHRB is "fundamentally lawful absent proof of misuse." The prosecution conceded: "Ich war nicht dabei" (I wasn't there) — acknowledging inability to disprove the dyeing claim. Full acquittal [7].

### Acquittal: AG Freiburg (May 2024)

The defendant was charged with possessing DMT based on an order of 500g of MHRB powder. The product had been advertised on the shop's website as a dyeing agent. The defendant stated he had used it to dye pants. The Amtsgericht Freiburg acquitted him after conducting full evidentiary proceedings, despite the prosecution requesting a conviction [17][18].

### Acquittal: AG Köln (case 583 Ls 47/20)

The Amtsgericht Köln issued a comparable acquittal to AG Rostock. The defendant had acquired 1,000g of MHRB. The court had serious doubts whether the defendant knew the plant contained DMT or could recognize that the quantity constituted a "nicht geringe Menge." Given the intended use the defendant pursued, these circumstances did not have to be apparent to him. Full acquittal [6].

### Dismissal: Stern Strafrecht case

A defendant was accused of ordering 100g of MHRB online. Defense attorney Stern challenged both the identity of the purchaser and the evidence regarding actual delivery contents, quantity, and potency. The prosecution terminated proceedings under § 170(2) StPO due to insufficient grounds [16].

## Sentencing: What Actually Happens When You Are Convicted

The sections above describe how investigations begin and how some cases result in acquittals or dismissals. But what happens when someone *is* convicted for DMT possession? This section compiles what is known about sentencing patterns — both DMT-specific and for BtMG offenses generally.

### The critical caveat: almost no DMT-specific sentencing data exists

DMT is a statistically rare substance in German criminal justice. The BKA's Polizeiliche Kriminalstatistik (PKS) does not break out DMT as a separate category — it is lumped into "Sonstige Betäubungsmittel" (other narcotics) or simply not tracked individually [25]. In the 2023 PKS, LSD accounted for only 0.2% (580 offenses) of all consumption-related drug offenses; DMT would be a fraction of even that [25]. The DBDD/REITOX 2024 Workbook on Drug Markets and Crime, the most comprehensive official source on German drug crime statistics, does not mention DMT, tryptamines, or ayahuasca at all [25].

This means there are no published statistics on "how DMT possession cases are typically sentenced." What follows is a reconstruction from (a) the few documented DMT/MHRB cases with known outcomes, (b) general BtMG sentencing statistics that apply to all Schedule I substances, and (c) attorney descriptions of typical outcomes.

**Pattern**: Of the documented MHRB/DMT cases where the defendant was a buyer (not a dealer), no personal-use conviction with a published sentence has been identified. The only convictions with known sentences involved dealing/distribution (LG Würzburg) or remain unpublished (LG Frankenthal). Multiple buyer cases resulted in acquittals or dismissals. This likely reflects selection bias — defense attorneys publicize favorable outcomes — but it also suggests that personal-use MHRB cases frequently do not result in harsh sentences.

### How cases end before trial: dismissals under § 31a BtMG, § 153/153a StPO, and § 170(2) StPO

For personal-use drug possession, German prosecutors have several options to end a case without trial:

**§ 31a BtMG — Absehen von der Verfolgung (abstention from prosecution)**: Prosecutors *may* decline to prosecute when the offender's guilt is minor, there is no public interest in prosecution, and the drugs were for personal consumption in small quantities. However, this provision has been **primarily applied to cannabis** in most German states (Bundesländer). For Schedule I substances other than cannabis (which includes DMT), most states do not routinely apply § 31a [28]. Some states (Baden-Württemberg, Bavaria) effectively do not apply it to any hard drug; others (Bremen, Hamburg, Schleswig-Holstein) allow it for small amounts of various substances [28]. No published data exists on § 31a being applied specifically to DMT cases.

**§ 153 StPO — Einstellung wegen Geringfügigkeit (dismissal for triviality)**: Prosecutors can dismiss cases where the offender's guilt is minor and there is no public interest. No conditions imposed.

**§ 153a StPO — Einstellung gegen Auflagen (conditional dismissal)**: Prosecutors dismiss the case on the condition that the defendant pays a sum to a charitable organization or the state treasury, performs community service, or meets other conditions. This is a common resolution for first-time, small-quantity drug possession. The advantage is that it does not result in a criminal conviction or Führungszeugnis entry. Documented MHRB cases have resulted in this outcome, though specific conditions (amounts paid) are not published [27].

**§ 170(2) StPO — Einstellung mangels hinreichenden Tatverdachts (dismissal for insufficient evidence)**: Used when evidence is inadequate to sustain charges. This was the outcome in the Stern Strafrecht 100g MHRB case [16].

### If convicted: general BtMG sentencing statistics

Since DMT-specific data does not exist, the best available proxy is the overall BtMG sentencing data from the Statistisches Bundesamt, as reported in the DBDD/REITOX 2024 Workbook [25]:

**In 2022, 53,899 people were convicted of BtMG offenses in Germany. The sanctions broke down as follows:**

| Sanction type | Number | Percentage |
|---------------|--------|-----------|
| **Geldstrafe (fine)** | 39,461 | **73.2%** |
| **Freiheitsstrafe auf Bewährung (prison, suspended/probation)** | 9,841 | **18.3%** |
| **Freiheitsstrafe ohne Bewährung (actual imprisonment)** | ~4,597 | **~8.5%** |
| **Total Freiheitsstrafen** | 14,437 | 26.8% |

Key observations from the DBDD data [25]:

- **Fines dominate**: Nearly three-quarters (73.2%) of all BtMG convictions result in a fine, not imprisonment. This share has been **steadily increasing** — from 58.4% in 2005 to 76.2% in 2020.
- **Actual imprisonment is rare**: Only ~8.5% of BtMG convictions result in unsuspended prison sentences. This share has been **steadily decreasing** — from 15.7% in 2005 to 7.9% in 2020.
- **Most prison sentences are suspended**: Of the 14,437 prison sentences imposed, 68.2% (9,841) were suspended on probation (Bewährung). Only 31.8% resulted in actual incarceration.
- **These figures include all BtMG offenses** — dealing, trafficking, import, and possession alike. For personal-use possession alone, the proportion of fines would be even higher and imprisonment even lower.

### Strafbefehl (penalty order) — the most likely outcome for a personal-use conviction

For minor BtMG offenses that are not dismissed, the most common procedure is a **Strafbefehl** (penalty order) — a written conviction issued by a judge at the prosecutor's request, without a full trial. The defendant receives it by mail and has 14 days to file an objection (Einspruch); if no objection is filed, it becomes legally binding [29].

Penalty orders typically impose a **Geldstrafe in Tagessätzen** (fine measured in daily rates). The number of daily rates reflects the severity; the amount per daily rate reflects the defendant's income. For first-time, small-quantity BtMG possession, attorneys report typical fines in the range of **30–90 Tagessätze** [29][30].

The **90 Tagessätze threshold** is critical: a fine of 90 Tagessätze or less is classified as a "minor conviction" and does **not** appear in the standard Führungszeugnis (certificate of good conduct) after 3 years. Above 90 Tagessätze, the conviction remains visible longer and has more significant collateral consequences for employment and professional licenses [30].

For a defendant earning a moderate income (e.g., €60/day net), 60 Tagessätze would mean a total fine of €3,600. At 90 Tagessätze, it would be €5,400.

### If the "nicht geringe Menge" threshold (3.6g DMT) is exceeded

When quantities exceed the threshold, the charge escalates from § 29 BtMG to § 29a BtMG, with a mandatory minimum of 1 year imprisonment. The sentencing outcomes change dramatically compared to § 29 cases.

#### Sentencing statistics for § 29a convictions

The most detailed published breakdown comes from the DBDD 2008 Selected Issue on Sentencing Statistics, using 2006 Destatis data (old federal states only) [32]. The REITOX 2024 Workbook provides more recent conviction counts but without the sentence-type breakdown [25].

**Convictions by offense severity and sentence type (2006, old federal states):**

| | § 29 (general offenses) | § 29a (nicht geringe Menge) | § 30 (import n.g.M.) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Total convicted** | 33,230 | 4,968 | 2,326 |
| **Fine only** | 25,278 (**76.1%**) | 59 (**1.2%**) | 34 (**1.5%**) |
| **Custodial sentence** | 7,952 (23.9%) | 4,909 (**98.8%**) | 2,292 (98.5%) |
| — suspended on probation | 5,541 (69.7% of custodial) | 2,986 (**60.8%** of custodial) | 993 (43.3% of custodial) |
| — actual imprisonment | ~2,411 (**7.3%** of all) | ~1,923 (**38.7%** of all) | ~1,299 (**55.8%** of all) |

Source: Statistisches Bundesamt 2007, as reported in DBDD 2008 [32]. Data covers only the old federal states (west Germany); new states were not included in the Strafverfolgungsstatistik at the time.

**Key takeaways:**

- **The jump from § 29 to § 29a is dramatic.** Under § 29, 76% of convictions result in a fine. Under § 29a, 99% result in a custodial sentence. A fine under § 29a is extremely rare (1.2% — likely the most favorable minder schwerer Fall outcomes).
- **However, most § 29a custodial sentences are suspended on probation (60.8%).** This means the convicted person does not go to prison but serves probation (typically 2–5 years). Only ~39% of § 29a convictions result in actual imprisonment.
- **Import (§ 30) is treated more harshly.** Only 43% of custodial sentences are suspended; 56% of all § 30 convictions result in actual imprisonment.
- These figures include **all substances and all offense types** within each paragraph (dealing, possession, production). There is no substance-specific breakdown.

The § 29a-specific pattern from 2006 (almost no fines, ~60% suspended, ~39% actual imprisonment) likely still holds, as the statutory framework has not changed.

#### What this means in practice

- Courts can classify the case as a **"minder schwerer Fall" (less severe case)** under § 29a Abs. 2 BtMG, which reduces the sentencing range to 3 months to 5 years [31]. This is particularly relevant when the threshold is only slightly exceeded and the offense was for personal use. The 59 fine-only outcomes out of 4,968 § 29a convictions (1.2%) likely represent the most favorable minder schwerer Fall cases.
- Prison sentences of up to 2 years can be **suspended on probation** (§ 56 StGB). This means that even a conviction under § 29a does not necessarily result in actual imprisonment if the court finds a favorable prognosis [31]. The data confirms this: 60.8% of § 29a custodial sentences were suspended.
- **No published statistics exist on how often courts apply the minder schwerer Fall classification.** The Strafverfolgungsstatistik does not track this. Criminal defense attorneys describe it as achievable but requiring active argumentation — it is not automatically applied [30][31].
- The extent to which the threshold is exceeded matters significantly. A marginal exceedance (e.g., 4g DMT in ~200g MHRB) is treated very differently from a massive exceedance (e.g., 24g DMT in 1.6 kg) [31]. A BayObLG decision held that even a 2.5-fold threshold exceedance can qualify as "geringe Grenzwertüberschreitung" warranting minder schwerer Fall consideration [33].
- An analogous case from Berlin (not DMT, but relevant for § 29a sentencing patterns): a defendant convicted of possession of a nicht geringe Menge of cannabis (115.5g with 10.78g THC), amphetamine (~3g), and MDMA (22 tablets) received a fine of **90 Tagessätze** after successful argument for a minder schwerer Fall — avoiding both imprisonment and a lasting Führungszeugnis entry [30].

### Comparison with other Schedule I substances

DMT is classified alongside LSD, psilocybin, and psilocin in BtMG Anlage I. The "nicht geringe Menge" thresholds for comparison:

| Substance | Nicht geringe Menge threshold | Source |
|-----------|------------------------------|--------|
| DMT | 3.6g base (~120 doses at 30mg) | LG Frankenthal 2012, LG Würzburg 2022 [4][26] |
| LSD | 6mg (~300 trips at 20µg) | BGH established |
| Psilocybin | 1.7g (~120 doses at ~14mg) | BGH established |
| Psilocin | 0.7g | BGH established |
| MDMA | 30g base | BGH established |
| Amphetamine | 10g base | BGH established |
| Cocaine | 5g base | BGH established |
| Heroin | 1.5g base | BGH established |

Note: The DMT and psilocybin thresholds are based on Landgericht decisions and have not been confirmed by the BGH (Bundesgerichtshof, Federal Court of Justice), unlike most other thresholds [10].

For LSD and psilocybin personal-use cases, sentencing follows the same general BtMG pattern described above: small quantities for personal use predominantly result in fines or dismissals; imprisonment is reserved for larger quantities or dealing [25][28]. The 2023 PKS recorded only 580 consumption-related LSD offenses and an unknown (but very small) number of DMT offenses nationwide [25]. These substances are enforcement afterthoughts compared to cannabis (173,945 offenses), cocaine (23,254), or amphetamine (26,129) [25].

### What this means for the cluster headache patient scenario

A person who orders a small quantity of MHRB (e.g., under 100g) for personal use, has no prior criminal record, and whose case is not dismissed would most likely face:

1. **Most probable**: Case dismissal (§ 170(2), § 153, or § 153a StPO) — especially if quantity is small and there is no evidence of extraction or distribution intent
2. **If not dismissed**: A Strafbefehl with a fine of 30–90 Tagessätze (roughly €1,800–€5,400 for a moderate earner)
3. **Very unlikely**: Actual imprisonment — this would require large quantities, evidence of dealing, or significant prior criminal history

If the quantity exceeds the 3.6g DMT threshold (which can happen with as little as ~120–360g of MHRB depending on concentration), the legal situation becomes significantly more serious. The 2006 data shows that 98.8% of § 29a convictions result in custodial sentences (vs. only 24% under § 29). However, 60.8% of those custodial sentences are suspended on probation — meaning the person does not actually go to prison. A minder schwerer Fall classification is possible for personal use, but the defense becomes harder and the stakes substantially higher.

## The "Dyeing" Defense — What Courts Found Persuasive

### Legal basis

German law does not automatically criminalize possession of a plant that happens to contain a controlled substance. The critical element is whether the possessor intended to use the material for its psychoactive properties. Multiple courts have held that when MHRB was purchased and used as a textile dye, no offense under the BtMG occurred [6][7][17].

### Factors that supported acquittals

Drawing from the documented cases above, the following factors contributed to successful defenses:

- The product was marketed and advertised by the seller as a dye or colorant, not as a psychoactive substance [6][17]
- The defendant had no other drug paraphernalia or extraction equipment [7]
- No drug-related search history or extraction guides were found on the defendant's devices [7]
- The defendant had no prior drug-related offenses [7]
- The defendant's account of how they used the material was internally consistent [6][7]
- The prosecution could not affirmatively prove intent for intoxication [7]

### Does MHRB actually work as a dye?

Yes — but poorly. The Hamburg State Criminal Police Office (LKA) experimentally tested MHRB as a textile dye in 2022 (published 2024) [19]. Key findings:

- MHRB **can** dye cotton and wool, confirming the factual basis of the defense
- However, the resulting colors were **"rather pale brownish to beige"** — not the "vivid/dark red tones" advertised by online sellers
- Colors faded significantly after washing; cold-dyed wool retained no visible color after machine washing
- Both the dyeing process and the DMT extraction process can be performed without chemical knowledge or professional equipment

This is a double-edged finding: it confirms that dyeing is a real use (supporting the defense), but the underwhelming results may lead courts to question why someone would pay €87.90+ for a mediocre dye when better alternatives exist.

### Factors that undermined defenses

Based on legal analyses by German criminal defense attorneys:

- Large quantities inconsistent with plausible dyeing needs [5][8]
- Extraction guides, chemistry manuals, or drug-related internet searches found on the defendant's devices [5]
- Presence of other drug paraphernalia or controlled substances [8]
- Prior drug-related offenses on the defendant's record [8]
- Inconsistent or contradictory statements by the defendant [5]
- Repeat orders of the same material over time [8]

### The process is the punishment

Even defendants who are ultimately acquitted must endure the full investigation and potentially a trial. This means months of uncertainty, the possibility of a house search, attorney costs (though these may be reimbursed upon acquittal), and the stress of criminal proceedings. A cluster headache patient should weigh not just the probability of conviction, but the burden of the process itself.

## Scenario Risk Assessment

For the specific scenario — a German cluster headache patient with no drug history ordering MHRB from a Dutch online shop, claiming the purpose is dyeing:

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **Detection probability** | The primary risk is not customs interception of individual parcels (low probability for any single package) but vendor customer list seizures. If the shop is later raided — even years after purchase — buyer records may be obtained. This risk is unpredictable and applies retroactively. |
| **Quantity** | Critical variable. At the commonly assumed 1% DMT, ~360g hits the threshold — but forensic labs have measured up to 3%, where only ~120g would suffice. Smaller orders reduce both legal exposure and dyeing-defense implausibility. |
| **Criminal exposure if caught** | Real. A criminal investigation will be opened. Penalties range from dismissal/fine (best case) to imprisonment (worst case for large quantities). |
| **"Dyeing" defense viability** | Supported by multiple acquittals (Rostock, Geilenkirchen, Freiburg, Köln). Strongest when: small quantity, product marketed as dye, no contradicting evidence, no prior record. Weakened by the Schallenberg finding that MHRB produces only pale/mediocre colors. |
| **No prior drug record** | Significantly strengthens the defense position. |
| **Cluster headache diagnosis** | Not legally relevant. No medical exemption for DMT exists in German law. |
| **Repeat orders** | Each additional order increases cumulative detection risk and undermines the plausibility of a "dyeing" claim. |
| **Digital footprint** | If a house search occurs, any DMT-related content found on devices (extraction guides, forum posts, this guide) could undermine a dyeing defense. |
| **Time delay** | Investigations may begin years after purchase. Documented cases show 3-year gaps between order and police contact. |

## Evidence Quality Note

This analysis draws on published court decisions (LG Frankenthal, LG Würzburg, AG Rostock, AG Geilenkirchen, AG Freiburg, AG Köln), a forensic chemistry paper from the Hamburg State Criminal Police Office (LKA), the official DBDD/REITOX 2024 Workbook on Drug Markets and Crime (which provides federal sentencing statistics from Destatis), German criminal defense attorney analyses (which may have a pro-defense bias, as they serve as marketing material for legal services), ICEERS legal monitoring data, and online forum reports. Key limitations:

- **No DMT-specific sentencing statistics exist.** The BKA and Destatis do not track DMT as a separate substance category. All sentencing data presented is for BtMG offenses overall (all substances combined), which is dominated by cannabis cases. The actual sentencing pattern for DMT personal use cannot be directly measured.
- No systematic data on customs interception rates for intra-EU parcels exists publicly
- Acquittal cases may be over-represented in publicly available sources, as defense attorneys publicize favorable outcomes. **No published personal-use DMT conviction with a specific sentence has been identified** — this likely reflects both selection bias (attorneys publish wins) and the rarity of DMT prosecutions, not necessarily that convictions never occur.
- The "1,000+ investigations" figure comes from a single attorney source; forum users have questioned its accuracy
- The 3.6g DMT threshold has been set by two Landgericht decisions (Frankenthal 2012, Würzburg 2022) but has not been confirmed by the BGH (Federal Court of Justice)
- DMT concentration in MHRB varies far more than commonly assumed: literature cites 0.57–1.0%, but forensic labs have measured up to 3.01%, making quantity-based risk estimates highly approximate
- The vendor customer list seizure pattern (Saarbrücken eBay shop, Dutch online shop) is documented through forum reports and attorney analyses, not through official court records detailing the investigation trigger. Extensive searching of German and English-language forums, attorney publications, Europol/Eurojust press releases, and court databases found no additional documented cases of foreign MHRB vendor customer lists reaching German authorities beyond the single Dutch shop case. Two additional JustAnswer.de threads describe buyers ordering from Dutch shops and facing police contact, but are behind paywalls and the investigation trigger is unclear [34][35]
- The general BtMG sentencing statistics (73.2% fines, 8.5% actual imprisonment) include dealing and trafficking convictions alongside personal use. For personal-use-only offenses, the proportion receiving fines would be higher and imprisonment lower, but the exact breakdown is not publicly available by offense subtype
