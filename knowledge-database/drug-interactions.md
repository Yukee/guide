# Drug Interactions with DMT

The primary safety concern with DMT use is drug interactions, particularly with medications that affect the serotonin system. This entry covers serotonin syndrome, and specific interactions with MAOIs, lithium, SSRIs/SNRIs, triptans, and other serotonergic substances.

## Serotonin Syndrome

### Mechanism
Serotonin syndrome occurs when excess serotonin accumulates in the central nervous system. Wikipedia describes it as resulting from "increased serotonin synthesis or release; reduced serotonin uptake or metabolism; and direct serotonin receptor activation" [1]. The dangerous effects — particularly "severe hypertonicity and hyperthermia" — are "primarily mediated by the activation of 5-HT₂A receptors at higher serotonin concentrations" [2].

### Symptoms
The Cleveland Clinic describes serotonin syndrome as a triad of "mental status changes, autonomic instability, and neuromuscular hyperactivity" [3].

**Severe symptoms** (medical emergency): seizures, high fever (>38.5°C / 101.3°F), severe muscle rigidity, uncontrollable eye movements (clonus), rapid/irregular heartbeat, loss of consciousness [1][3].

**Milder symptoms**: agitation, restlessness, rapid heartbeat, shivering, sweating, muscle twitching, dilated pupils, nausea, diarrhea [1][3].

### Onset timing
"Clinical symptoms typically develop within 2 hours of an increase in dose or the addition of a serotonergic drug" [2]. However, with MAOIs, onset can be delayed (see below).

### Treatment
Serotonin syndrome is treatable. The Mayo Clinic states: "first-line management involves withdrawal of the offending serotonergic drugs and provision of supportive care" [4]. Benzodiazepines can control agitation and seizures. In severe cases, the serotonin antagonist cyproheptadine may be used [4]. "With quick diagnosis and treatment, most people see their symptoms go away completely within 24 to 72 hours" [3].

### Distinguishing from normal DMT effects
Normal DMT effects include faster heartbeat, sweating, and dilated pupils. These overlap with mild serotonin syndrome symptoms. The distinction lies in severity, combination of symptoms, and duration — DMT effects resolve within 15-20 minutes, while serotonin syndrome persists and may worsen.

## Lithium

### Seizure risk
Combining lithium with classic psychedelics carries a significant seizure risk. Nayak et al. (2021), analyzing online experience reports, found that "47% of 62 lithium plus psychedelic reports involved seizures, and an additional 18% resulted in bad trips" [5].

Broken down by substance: "Of the 55 reports of lithium plus LSD, 27 (49.1%) were seizures; of the 6 reports of lithium plus psilocybin, 2 (33.3%) were seizures; and the sole report of lithium plus DMT did not involve seizures" [5]. The researchers "provisionally conclude that psychedelic use may pose a significant seizure risk for patients on lithium" [5].

Severity was notable: "39% of lithium reports involved medical attention" [5].

### Specificity to lithium
This risk appears specific to lithium, not mood stabilizers generally: "None of 34 lamotrigine reports involved seizures" [5].

### Evidence quality
The primary evidence comes from analysis of online experience reports (Erowid, Reddit), which have "inherent limitations regarding verification and standardization" [5]. However, the effect size is large and the mechanism is plausible. No controlled clinical studies exist.

## MAOIs (Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors)

### Mechanism of interaction
MAOIs inhibit monoamine oxidase, the enzyme primarily responsible for breaking down DMT and serotonin. When DMT is combined with MAOIs, two things happen:

1. **DMT becomes dramatically more potent and longer-lasting.** MAOIs prevent the rapid breakdown of DMT by MAO-A, resulting in higher plasma levels and extended duration. One study found that "premedication of harmine significantly increased the elimination half-life of DMT to 36 minutes" [6] — compared to 9-12 minutes without MAOIs [7]. This is the principle behind ayahuasca, where a "mild 10-20 minute experience can become 2-4 hours" [8].

2. **Serotonin syndrome risk increases.** With MAO inhibited, both DMT and endogenous serotonin accumulate. However, the clinical picture is nuanced — ayahuasca itself contains MAOIs (harmine/harmaline are reversible MAO-A inhibitors) and has been used safely in ceremonial contexts for centuries. Researchers note that "only at extreme doses can the reversible MAOIs in ayahuasca evoke the nearly complete inhibition that may be a precondition for serotonin syndrome" [9].

### Types of MAOIs
- **Prescription MAOIs:** Phenelzine (Nardil), tranylcypromine (Parnate), isocarboxazid (Marplan), selegiline (Emsam)
- **Linezolid:** An oxazolidinone antibiotic that is "a reversible monoamine oxidase inhibitor (MAOI) that inhibits the action of monoamine oxidase A" [10]. The FDA has issued specific warnings about "serious CNS reactions possible when linezolid (Zyvox) is given to patients taking certain psychiatric medications" [11].
- **Natural MAOIs:** Harmine and harmaline in ayahuasca (*Banisteriopsis caapi*), Syrian rue (*Peganum harmala*), and changa blends [8].

### Delayed onset concern
Because MAOIs slow serotonin metabolism, serotonin syndrome symptoms may develop later than expected — "hours after taking DMT" rather than the typical 2-hour window [2].

## SSRIs and SNRIs

### Mechanism
SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) and SNRIs (serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors) increase serotonin levels by blocking its reuptake from the synaptic cleft [1].

### Risk assessment
The serotonin syndrome risk from combining DMT (without MAOIs) with a single SSRI or SNRI appears low. A systematic review of psychedelic drug interactions found that "classic psychedelic serotonin agonists (LSD, DMT and psilocybin) appear to be the safest in combination with other psychiatric medications and seem to have a negligible risk of serotonin toxicity" [12]. More specifically, "there is likely little risk of combining SSRI/SNRIs with classic psychedelics like psilocybin, LSD and DMT" [12].

However, the risk is not zero, and increases with:
- Multiple serotonergic drugs taken simultaneously
- Higher doses of either substance
- Combination with DMT in ayahuasca form (where the MAOI component adds significant risk)

### Fluoxetine (Prozac) washout concern
Fluoxetine is uniquely long-lasting among SSRIs. Its half-life is "4 to 6 days after chronic administration," and its active metabolite norfluoxetine has a half-life of "4 to 16 days" [13]. The FDA labeling states: "At least 5 weeks should be allowed after stopping Prozac before starting an MAOI" [13][14]. Active drug substance can remain in the body for "weeks to months after discontinuation" [13].

Other SSRIs have much shorter half-lives — "five half-lives equates to about five days for most SSRIs except fluoxetine" [14].

### Discontinuation warning
Stopping antidepressants without medical supervision can cause withdrawal syndromes. Any changes to antidepressant regimens should be discussed with a prescribing physician.

## Triptans

### Serotonin syndrome risk: low
Despite an FDA warning issued in 2006 about serotonin syndrome risk with triptans combined with SSRIs/SNRIs, subsequent evidence has been largely reassuring.

An analysis of the 29 cases underlying the FDA alert found that "10 cases actually met the Sternbach Criteria for diagnosing serotonin syndrome. No cases fulfilled the Hunter Criteria for serotonin toxicity" [15].

Large retrospective studies provide stronger evidence: "a 14-year electronic health records study looked at data from 19,017 patients who had concurrently received a triptan and an SNRI or SSRI. Just 2 patients were classified as having definite serotonin syndrome" [16]. The American Headache Society position paper concluded that "the currently available evidence does not support limiting the use of triptans with SSRIs or SNRIs" [17].

### Cardiovascular interaction
The more relevant concern when combining triptans with DMT is cardiovascular. Triptans constrict blood vessels (this is their therapeutic mechanism for migraines), and DMT raises blood pressure and heart rate. The combined cardiovascular stress is the primary concern.

### Recommended wait times
- **Most triptans** (sumatriptan, rizatriptan, zolmitriptan, etc.): half-lives of 2-6 hours. A 24-hour wait provides >4 half-lives of clearance [18].
- **Frovatriptan (Frova):** Has "by far the longest half-life time (26 hours), whereas all the other oral triptans have a half-life time between 2 and 6 hours" [18]. A 5-day wait (~5 half-lives) is recommended.

## Other Serotonergic Substances

### Serotonergic opioids
Not all opioids are serotonergic. The key distinction:

**High serotonin risk:** Tramadol, meperidine (pethidine), and dextromethorphan (DXM) "are high-risk opioids for serotonin syndrome when used with serotonergic antidepressants" [19]. Tramadol "inhibits SERT" and has "a direct serotonin-releasing action" [19]. Fentanyl and methadone also have serotonergic properties (medium risk) [19].

**Low serotonin risk:** "Morphine is not expected to interact with antidepressant medicines to cause serotonin syndrome" [20]. Clinical guidance recommends "using a non-synthetic opioid like morphine" when opioids are needed alongside serotonergic medications [20].

### Dextromethorphan (DXM)
DXM is a serotonin reuptake inhibitor found in many OTC cold and cough medicines (e.g., Robitussin DM, NyQuil, Delsym). It is classified as high-risk for serotonin syndrome when combined with other serotonergic agents [19].

### St. John's Wort
St. John's Wort (*Hypericum perforatum*) has serotonin reuptake inhibiting properties and carries "moderate risk with SSRIs and MAOIs due to its inhibition of serotonin reuptake and metabolism" [19][21].

### Chlorpheniramine and brompheniramine
These first-generation antihistamines are unexpectedly serotonergic. Chlorpheniramine "acts as a serotonin reuptake inhibitor with a Kd of 15.2 nM for the serotonin transporter" and "compares favorably with amitriptyline and imipramine with respect to actions on both serotonergic and noradrenergic neurons" [22]. Its serotonergic potency was discovered by Nobel Laureate Arvid Carlsson as early as 1969 [22]. Brompheniramine (Dimetapp) is a structurally related halogenated pheniramine derivative with likely similar serotonergic properties [22].

### Supplemental oxygen
There is no known interaction between inhaled DMT and supplemental oxygen. Many cluster headache patients use high-flow oxygen as a first-line abortive treatment, and there is no pharmacological basis for concern about concurrent use.

## Risk Stacking

The risk of serotonin syndrome increases when multiple serotonergic substances are combined. Each individual drug may seem safe, but their combined effect on serotonin levels can be additive or synergistic. Patients should inventory all medications, supplements, and OTC drugs for serotonergic properties before using DMT.
