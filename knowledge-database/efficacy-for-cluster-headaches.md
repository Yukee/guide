# DMT Efficacy for Cluster Headaches

DMT has emerged as a reported acute abortive treatment for cluster headache attacks. The evidence is currently limited to patient reports, community surveys, and a small number of case reports — no randomized controlled trials have been published as of early 2026.

## Cluster Headaches and the Serotonin System

### Pathophysiology
Cluster headache involves dysregulation of multiple neurotransmitter systems, including serotonin (5-HT). A 2025 systematic review documented "altered signaling... for several mediators, including calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP), pituitary adenylate cyclase-activating polypeptide (PACAP), vasoactive intestinal peptide (VIP), serotonin (5-HT), hypocretin/orexin, and nitric-oxide-related pathways" [1].

The hypothalamus plays a central role: neuroimaging shows "activation in the ipsilateral inferior hypothalamic grey matter" during attacks [2][3]. The circadian rhythmicity of attacks — commonly peaking around 2 AM [4] — implicates the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), the brain's master clock located in the hypothalamus [4][5].

### Serotonin's role
The established efficacy of sumatriptan (a serotonin 5-HT₁B/₁D agonist) and the structural kinship between triptans and DMT (both tryptamines) provides a pharmacological rationale for DMT's aborting effect. "5-HT₁B agonism likely causes the constriction of cranial vessels, which inhibits trigeminal neuropeptide release like CGRP" [6]. DMT, while acting on different serotonin receptor subtypes (primarily 5-HT₂A), engages the same broader serotonin system.

### Nocturnal timing
"The most common time of day for an attack was 2 AM in three large studies" [4]. This nocturnal predilection is linked to disrupted melatonin rhythms: "the nocturnal peak in melatonin levels is reduced or even absent in cluster headache patients during an attack period" [4].

## Evidence for DMT as an Acute Abortive

### Patient reports
Multiple independent sources describe inhaled DMT aborting cluster headache attacks within seconds:

- **Bob Wold** (President, Clusterbusters): "One inhalation will end the attack for most people... It could end that attack in less than a minute" [7].
- **Yiftach Yerushalmy** (patient): "DMT, the smallest microdose of maybe 5mg with a vape pen stops the worst pain known, in literally 10–20 seconds" [7].
- **Joe McKay** (patient, retired NYC firefighter): "Having a DMT vape pen right next to my bedside allowed me to hit that pen and within a minute or two I'm closing my eyes and going back to sleep" [7].
- One patient reported returning all oxygen equipment after years of reliance, now aborting "every single attack in seconds" [7].

### Pattern of pain resolution
When DMT aborts an attack, patients consistently describe the pain vanishing sharply — not a gradual fade. One patient described "feeling the pain fizzle away and die in a matter of seconds" [7]. This sharp resolution pattern is distinct from the slower relief seen with oxygen therapy.

### Dosing pattern
Patients report using DMT multiple times per day to abort attacks as they occur, rather than as a preventive. It is used as-needed, similar to how sumatriptan or oxygen is used. Patients typically wait 30-45 seconds between inhalations to assess effectiveness before taking additional doses [7].

### Survey data
The ClusterBusters Medication Use Survey (Schindler et al., 2015) of 496 participants found that "indoleamine hallucinogens, psilocybin, lysergic acid diethylamide, and lysergic acid amide, were comparable to or more efficacious than most conventional medications" [8]. DMT was not separately analyzed in this survey due to limited respondents at that time.

A Swedish cohort study (Smedfors et al., 2024) noted that "only one study participant reported use of DMT and this with full abortive effect" [9].

## Evidence Limitations

### No clinical trials
As of early 2026, "there exist no published studies on the efficacy of DMT to treat cluster headaches" in controlled clinical settings [6]. A survey study at Yale, led by Dr. Emmanuelle Schindler ("arguably the leading researcher on the topic of psychedelics for cluster headache" [7]), is currently assessing DMT efficacy.

### Anecdotal evidence base
The evidence comes primarily from patient self-reports gathered through community organizations (ClusterBusters), online forums (Reddit r/clusterheads), and advocacy publications. While consistent across multiple independent sources, these reports lack the controls of clinical research — no placebo comparison, no blinding, potential reporting bias toward positive outcomes.

### Mechanism uncertainty
The precise mechanism by which inhaled DMT aborts cluster headache attacks within seconds is not established. Possibilities include:
- Direct serotonin receptor modulation in the trigeminal system
- Rapid vasomotor effects
- Modulation of hypothalamic circuitry
- Other as-yet-unidentified pathways
