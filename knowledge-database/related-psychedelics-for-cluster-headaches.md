# Related Psychedelics for Cluster Headaches

Several tryptamine and lysergamide psychedelics besides DMT have shown promise for cluster headache treatment, primarily for cycle prevention ("busting") rather than acute attack aborting. Researchers have also begun studying DMT and related molecules for depression and PTSD.

## Psilocybin

### Evidence
Psilocybin has the strongest evidence base among psychedelics for cluster headache, including the first randomized controlled trial.

**Schindler et al. (2022)** — First RCT of psilocybin for cluster headache. 14 participants (6 placebo, 8 psilocybin) received a low-dose pulse regimen. "The change in cluster attack frequency was 0.03 attacks/week with placebo and -3.2 attacks/week with psilocybin" — not statistically significant overall (p = 0.251), but the "effect size was small in episodic participants (d = 0.35) but large in chronic participants (d = 1.25)" [1].

**Extension phase (2024):** "A patient-informed psilocybin pulse regimen significantly reduced cluster attack frequency by approximately 50%... regardless of individual response to psilocybin in the first round" [2].

### Mechanism
Notably, "headache frequency changes after psilocybin administration were independent from either acute psychedelic effects or measures of mental health" [3], suggesting the therapeutic benefit comes from direct neurological effects rather than the psychological experience.

### ClusterBusters survey
The community survey of 496 participants found that "indoleamine hallucinogens, psilocybin, lysergic acid diethylamide, and lysergic acid amide, were comparable to or more efficacious than most conventional medications" for cluster headache [4]. Among those using tryptamines, "68% of respondents... had a 4 or 5 out of 5 relief" [4].

### Safety
"Psilocybin was well-tolerated without any unexpected or serious adverse events" in the cluster headache trial [1].

## LSD (Lysergic Acid Diethylamide)

### Evidence
LSD has been reported effective for cluster headache prevention in patient surveys and case reports. The ClusterBusters survey included LSD alongside psilocybin, with similar efficacy ratings [4].

### BOL-148 (2-Bromo-LSD)
A non-hallucinogenic LSD derivative has been tested for cluster headache prevention. Karst et al. (2010) conducted an open case series with 5 patients receiving three single doses of BOL-148. Results: "safely improved CH cycles or significantly improved frequency/intensity of episodes in four out of five patients" [5]. Outcomes included "a six-month remission period, pronounced reduction of attack frequency with transition from chronic to episodic form, and... nine months of ongoing remission with only sporadic attacks" [5].

Critically, "no visual hallucinations or distortions occurred, nor was there any evidence of delusional thinking or overt psychosis" and "no changes to heart rate and blood pressure were observed" [5]. This finding is significant because it suggests the anti-cluster headache effect of these compounds may not require the psychedelic experience — consistent with Schindler's psilocybin finding [3].

### Mechanism
BOL-148's "primary pharmacological action relevant to headache is its partial agonism at the serotonin 2A (5-HT₂A) receptor" [6]. The fact that a non-hallucinogenic 5-HT₂A partial agonist retains anti-cluster headache efficacy narrows the likely mechanism of action.

## 5-MeO-DALT (N,N-Diallyl-5-methoxytryptamine)

### Evidence
5-MeO-DALT is a synthetic tryptamine that has been adopted by some cluster headache patients as a prophylactic treatment.

In 2014, two cluster headache patients conducted a self-experiment: "the results of this trial demonstrated that 5-MeO-DALT could be extremely effective in preventing cluster headache attacks" [7]. A subsequent survey of 46 patients found that "87% of patients got 'positive effects' from 5-MeO-DALT taken orally" [7].

One patient reported: "This is the least amount of pain I've been in for a year and the most I've felt myself for the same amount of time. It's so nice to factor in the 'bust' without having to lose a full evening because of the medicine's effects" [7] — referencing 5-MeO-DALT's milder psychoactive profile compared to psilocybin or LSD.

### Evidence quality
The evidence consists entirely of patient surveys and case reports. No clinical trials have been conducted.

## DMT and Related Molecules for Other Conditions

### Depression
Research is rapidly advancing:

- **Phase IIa RCT (Nature Medicine, 2025):** "A single 10-minute intravenous infusion of DMT with psychological support led to a statistically significant reduction in depressive symptoms" for major depressive disorder, with effects "observed as early as 1 week following the session and, in some cases, lasting for up to 3 months" [8].

- **Vaporized DMT for treatment-resistant depression (2025):** A phase 2a trial showed "an average reduction of 21.14 points on the Montgomery-Asberg Depression Rating Scale by day 7," with a "response rate of 85.71% and remission rate of 57.14%... lasting up to 3 months" [9].

- Multiple companies are developing DMT formulations for depression, with researchers anticipating "the first DMT-based treatment could be approved within the next 3-4 years" [10].

### PTSD
Observational data exists primarily for 5-MeO-DMT (a related but distinct compound). A study of 86 Special Operations Forces Veterans who received "ibogaine followed by 5-MeO-DMT at a residential clinic in Mexico" showed "improvements in PTSD symptoms, depression, anxiety, and life satisfaction" [11].

## Summary: Evidence Levels

| Compound | Use | Evidence Level | Key Finding |
|----------|-----|---------------|-------------|
| Psilocybin | Preventive (busting) | RCT (small) | ~50% reduction in attack frequency |
| LSD | Preventive (busting) | Survey + case reports | Comparable to psilocybin in surveys |
| BOL-148 | Preventive | Open case series (n=5) | 4/5 patients improved, no psychoactive effects |
| 5-MeO-DALT | Preventive | Patient survey (n=46) | 87% reported positive effects |
| DMT | Acute aborting | Patient reports only | Reported to abort attacks in seconds |
| DMT | Depression | Phase IIa RCTs | Significant antidepressant effects |
