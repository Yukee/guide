# Pharmacology and Mechanism of Action of DMT

N,N-Dimethyltryptamine (DMT) is a substituted tryptamine and indole alkaloid (chemical formula C₁₂H₁₆N₂, molecular mass 188.27) that occurs naturally in many plants and animals [1][2]. It was first synthesized in 1931 by Canadian chemist Richard Helmuth Fredrick Manske, and its psychoactive properties were discovered in 1956 by Hungarian chemist Stephen Szára [1].

## Chemical Classification: The Tryptamine Family

DMT belongs to the **tryptamine** family, compounds built around an indole ring (a fused pyrrole-benzene bicyclic structure) joined to an amino group via an ethyl sidechain [3]. Serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine, 5-HT) is itself a tryptamine [3][4].

Sumatriptan and other triptans are also tryptamine derivatives. As Wikipedia states, triptans including "sumatriptan, rizatriptan, eletriptan, almotriptan, zolmitriptan, contain the psychedelic drug dimethyltryptamine (DMT) within their chemical structures and hence are derivatives of DMT" [5]. However, despite the structural kinship, triptans and DMT have very different pharmacological profiles: triptans selectively agonize 5-HT₁B and 5-HT₁D receptors to constrict cranial blood vessels [5], while DMT acts as a non-selective agonist across many serotonin receptor subtypes [6].

## Natural Occurrence

### In plants
DMT is found in numerous plant species. The best-known DMT-containing plants include *Psychotria viridis* (chacruna) and *Mimosa hostilis* (jurema), used in traditional ayahuasca and analogous preparations [1][7].

### In the human body
DMT is an endogenous compound. It is "biosynthesized in the human body from the essential amino acid tryptophan" via decarboxylation to tryptamine, followed by N-methylation by the enzyme indolethylamine-N-methyltransferase (INMT) [8]. INMT is "widely expressed in the body, primarily in peripheral tissue such as the lungs, thyroid and adrenal gland" and at intermediate levels in the placenta, skeletal muscle, heart, small intestine, stomach, retina, pancreas, and lymph nodes [8].

DMT has been detected in human blood, urine, and cerebrospinal fluid [1][8]. In a 2019 study, Barker et al. demonstrated that "the rat brain is capable of synthesizing and releasing DMT" and that it is "present in the brain at levels akin to canonic neurotransmitters like serotonin and dopamine" [8][9]. The physiological role of endogenous DMT remains incompletely understood [8].

## Traditional Use in Ayahuasca

Ayahuasca is a psychoactive brew traditionally made from *Banisteriopsis caapi* vine (containing the MAOIs harmine, harmaline, and tetrahydroharmine) and a DMT-containing plant, usually *Psychotria viridis* [7]. Archaeological evidence suggests the traditional use of psychoactive plants in South America dates "as far back as 900 B.C. in artwork and paraphernalia from the Chavin people of Peru" [10]. The specific ritual use of ayahuasca was widespread among Indigenous groups across the Amazon basin (Peru, Brazil, Colombia, Ecuador) by the 19th century, though its precise origin is uncertain [7][10]. It is "regarded as a plant teacher with healing properties by various Indigenous groups, such as the Shipibo, employed by shamans for diagnosis and healing" [10].

## Mechanism of Action: Serotonin Receptors

DMT's psychoactive effects are primarily mediated through the serotonin system. It binds to "5-HT₁A, 5-HT₁B, 5-HT₁D, 5-HT₂A, 5-HT₂B, 5-HT₂C, 5-HT₅A, 5-HT₆ and 5-HT₇ receptors with affinities ranging from 39 nM to 2.1 μM" [6]. The primary psychedelic effects are attributed to agonism at the 5-HT₂A receptor [6][11].

Smith et al. (1998) demonstrated that in cells transfected with the 5-HT₂A or 5-HT₂C receptor, "DMT activated the major intracellular signaling pathway (phosphoinositide hydrolysis) to an extent comparable to that produced by serotonin" [11].

### Beyond serotonin

DMT's pharmacology extends well beyond serotonin receptors. It "interacts with ionotropic and metabotropic glutamate receptors, dopamine, acetylcholine, TAAR [trace amine-associated receptors], and sigma-1 receptors" [6]. It also acts as "a substrate of the serotonin uptake transporter (SERT) and the vesicle monoamine transporter (VMAT2)" [6].

### Serotonin's role in the body

Serotonin (5-HT) — the system DMT primarily acts upon — is a neurotransmitter with wide-ranging functions. It plays roles in "mood, memory, anger, fear, appetite, stress, addiction, sexual pleasure, sleep, pain perception, cerebral vascular tone, and central respiratory drive" [12][13]. About 90% of the body's serotonin is produced in the gastrointestinal tract [13]. In the brain, serotonergic projections from the raphe nuclei are involved in regulating mood and emotion; serotonin is also a precursor to melatonin, the hormone regulating the sleep-wake cycle [13]. Descending serotonergic projections form a pain-inhibiting pathway "that may be relevant to disorders such as fibromyalgia, migraine, and other pain disorders" [13].

## Freebase vs. Salt Forms

DMT exists in two forms relevant to administration:

- **Freebase DMT:** The pure, non-ionized form. Boiling point approximately 146-147°C at 10 mmHg [14][15]. This is the form used for vaporization.
- **Salt forms** (e.g., DMT fumarate, DMT hydrochloride): Ionic forms with higher melting points. DMT fumarate melts at ~152°C [14]. Salt forms are more stable for storage but do not vaporize efficiently — they tend to decompose rather than cleanly transition to vapor.

As noted in the patent literature, "the freebase form, although less stable than DMT hemifumarate, is favored by recreational users choosing to vaporize the chemical as it has a lower boiling point" resulting in "a cooler, less harsh vapor" [15].
