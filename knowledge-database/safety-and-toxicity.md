# Safety and Toxicity Profile of DMT

DMT's safety profile is characterized by low physical toxicity, no addiction potential, transient cardiovascular effects, and rare serious adverse events in clinical settings. The primary risks relate to drug interactions and psychological effects in vulnerable populations.

## Addiction and Dependence

### No physical dependence
DMT does not produce physical dependence, withdrawal symptoms, or compulsive drug-seeking behavior. The JAMA Psychiatry meta-analysis (2024) found that classic psychedelics including DMT are "generally well tolerated" and lack the dependence potential of stimulants, opioids, and alcohol [1][2].

A narrative review by Schlag et al. (2022) "critically examined the evidence for potential harms of psychedelics, such as abuse liability, potential for dependence, toxicity, and overdose" and concluded that "most of these risks are minimal or unsupported by the available evidence" [3].

### Drug harm rankings
In David Nutt's landmark Lancet study (2010), which ranked 20 drugs on 16 harm criteria (9 individual, 7 societal), psychedelics including LSD and psilocybin (mushrooms) "were placed at the lower end of the harm spectrum, indicating that they pose less risk than many commonly used substances, including alcohol and tobacco" [4][5]. While DMT was not individually ranked, it shares the same pharmacological class.

### Abuse liability nuance
Winstock et al. (2014) noted a nuanced picture: while "the dependence potential of oral DMT and the risk of sustained psychological disturbance are minimal," they argued that "the very desirable effect profile of smoked DMT indicates a high abuse liability which may be offset by a low urge to use more" [6]. However, this was described as a theoretical concern, not an observed clinical problem.

## Physical Toxicity

### Lethal dose
Animal studies suggest a large safety margin. Gable (2007) found that "a lethal dose of [DMT] in humans is probably greater than 20 times the typical ceremonial dose" [7]. The LD50 in mice is 47 mg/kg intraperitoneally and 32 mg/kg intravenously [7][8].

### Organ toxicity
No organ damage has been documented at typical human doses. White et al. (2024) found no reports of toxicity from long-term DMT use in their systematic review [9]. In animal studies with extremely high doses (4x and 8x typical), some rats died (44% and 52%) and survivors showed kidney injury and hippocampal neuronal loss — but these doses were far above any human use range [8].

## Cardiovascular Effects

DMT produces dose-dependent, transient increases in blood pressure and heart rate.

### Clinical data
Strassman et al. (1994) administered IV DMT at 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, and 0.4 mg/kg to 11 volunteers and found that "DMT dose-dependently elevated blood pressure, heart rate, pupil diameter, and rectal temperature" [10]. Systolic blood pressure increases reached approximately 30-40 mmHg and diastolic increases around 20 mmHg at the highest dose [10][11].

A phase I clinical trial of inhaled DMT found "a mild, transient, and self-limited increase in blood pressure and heart rate" described as "transient, non-clinically significant sympathomimetic effects" [12].

### At aborting doses (5-10 mg inhaled)
Aborting doses are substantially lower than doses used in most clinical studies (which typically test 0.2-0.4 mg/kg IV, equivalent to roughly 15-30 mg for a 70 kg person). At 5-10 mg inhaled, cardiovascular effects would be expected to be modest and brief.

### Higher doses
Even at higher doses, cardiovascular effects are temporary (resolving within 15-20 minutes) and have not produced cardiovascular emergencies in clinical trials. However, researchers have explored "coformulations with short-acting beta-blockers or nitrates, matched to DMT's pharmacokinetics, to mitigate peripheral cardiovascular risks" for therapeutic applications [13], indicating the cardiovascular effects are taken seriously as a clinical consideration.

### One notable case
White et al. (2024) documented one case of "asymptomatic bradycardia and hypotension" in a female participant receiving IV DMT at 0.3 mg/kg, "though prompt intervention prevented complications" [9].

## Psychological Safety

### At aborting doses
At 5-10 mg inhaled, psychological effects are mild: enhanced colors, body sensations, and altered perception while maintaining full awareness and cognitive function (see [subjective-effects.md](subjective-effects.md) for details). These doses are well below the threshold for the intense psychedelic experiences studied in clinical trials.

### Adverse psychological events in clinical trials
The JAMA Psychiatry meta-analysis (Hinkle et al., 2024) reviewed 214 studies with 3,504 total participants receiving classic psychedelics. Key finding: "Serious adverse events were reported for no healthy participants and for approximately 4% of participants with preexisting neuropsychiatric disorders; among these SAEs were worsening depression, suicidal behavior, psychosis, and convulsive episodes" [1].

A 2025 systematic review of DMT-specific early-phase clinical trials (healthy volunteers and MDD patients) found "no serious adverse events were reported across the various routes of administration" [14].

### Transience of effects
All psychological effects of inhaled/IV DMT resolve completely within 15-20 minutes. There is no mechanism by which a person can become "stuck" in a DMT experience — the compound is rapidly metabolized by MAO-A (see [pharmacokinetics.md](pharmacokinetics.md)).

### Anxiety and difficult experiences
Some participants in clinical trials experience anxiety, especially at higher doses. A DMT trial for major depression reported that adverse events were "mostly mild to moderate, commonly infusion site pain, nausea and transient anxiety, with no serious adverse events" [15]. At aborting doses (5-10 mg), anxiety is uncommon.

## Long-Term Psychological Effects

### Population-level evidence (ayahuasca)
White et al. (2024) reported that a large observational survey of 10,836 ayahuasca users found "55.9% of all respondents reported adverse mental health effects in weeks or months following consumption," primarily involving "emotional-cognitive effects (42.0%) and altered perception effects (38.3%)." Importantly, these were "generally mild and transient and considered to be part of a positive process of psychological growth" [9]. Note: ayahuasca involves much higher DMT doses over much longer durations than aborting use.

### Psychosis risk in population studies
In approximately 25,000 ayahuasca sessions over five years in religious contexts, researchers documented "between 13 and 24 instances of unspecified psychotic incidents" — "a rate below .1%" [9][16]. Based on census data from the UDV (a Brazilian ayahuasca church), "the incidence of new psychotic episodes would be 10.8 per 100,000 person-years" [16].

A systematic review by Dos Santos et al. (2017) found that psychotic cases predominantly involved "individuals with current or prior psychiatric symptomatology, family history of psychosis, or those who used DMT concurrently with other drugs" [16].

## Contraindications

### Psychotic disorders
DMT is contraindicated in individuals with personal or family history of psychotic or bipolar spectrum disorders. Leading guidelines recommend that "individuals with personal or family history of schizophrenia or schizophreniform disorders, psychotic depression or mania, or with ongoing manic or psychotic symptomatology, should avoid ayahuasca/DMT intake" [16][17].

Persons with psychotic disorders are routinely excluded from all psychedelic clinical trials [1][16]. This exclusion means there is very limited data on the effects of DMT in this population, and the little that exists suggests elevated risk.

### Drug interactions
The main safety concern is interactions with serotonergic medications — particularly MAOIs, lithium, and SSRIs. See [drug-interactions.md](drug-interactions.md) for detailed coverage.

## Reporting Quality Caveat

The JAMA Psychiatry meta-analysis noted significant gaps in adverse event reporting across psychedelic studies: "only 23.5% of studies systematically assessed adverse events, and just 29.4% reported all adverse events rather than only adverse drug reactions" [1]. This means the evidence base, while reassuring, has blind spots.
