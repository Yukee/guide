# Legal Risk: Ordering Mimosa Hostilis Root Bark to Austria

This entry analyzes the legal risk of a specific scenario: an Austrian cluster headache patient orders Mimosa hostilis root bark (MHRB) from a Netherlands-based online shop. Austria occupies a distinctive legal position among the three countries analyzed in this series. Like Germany, Austria schedules only DMT (the molecule), not the plants that contain it — but Austria goes further in two critical respects. First, the official Viennese drug-checking service checkit! has explicitly stated that "DMT-haltige Pflanzen sind nicht im SMG erfasst" (plants containing DMT are not covered by the narcotics law). Second, Austrian law provides **mandatory prosecutorial diversion** for personal-use drug offenses: the prosecutor must defer charges, and successful completion of a 1–2 year probationary period results in no criminal record. The combination of an unscheduled plant and mandatory diversion makes Austria the most legally favorable of the three jurisdictions analyzed — though the absence of any court precedent on MHRB means this favorable position has never been tested.

For the international and US-focused legal context, see [legal-status-and-detection.md](legal-status-and-detection.md). For the German-specific analysis, see [legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md](legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md). For the French-specific analysis, see [legal-risk-france-mhrb.md](legal-risk-france-mhrb.md). For the UK-specific analysis, see [legal-risk-uk-mhrb.md](legal-risk-uk-mhrb.md).

## Austrian Legal Framework

### DMT under Austrian law

DMT (dimethyltryptamine) is classified as a Suchtgift (narcotic drug) under Austrian law, listed in the Suchtgiftverordnung (SV — Narcotic Drugs Regulation, BGBl. II Nr. 374/1997) under the chemical designation "3-(2-(Dimethylamino)-ethyl)indol" [1][2]. All salts, ethers, esters, racemates, and isomers are also controlled. The Suchtgiftverordnung implements Austria's obligations under the 1971 UN Convention on Psychotropic Substances, under which DMT appears in Schedule I [1].

The criminal provisions governing narcotics offenses are contained in the Suchtmittelgesetz (SMG — Narcotic Substances Act), which entered into force on January 1, 1998, replacing the former Suchtgiftgesetz (SGG) [3][4]. The SMG covers all narcotics (Suchtgifte), psychotropic substances (psychotrope Stoffe), and precursor chemicals (Vorläuferstoffe).

### MHRB's legal status — the checkit! position

This is the single most important legal finding for this analysis. **Mimosa hostilis is not listed by name in the Suchtgiftverordnung.** Unlike France — which explicitly scheduled Mimosa hostilis, Banisteriopsis caapi, Psychotria viridis, and other ayahuasca-related plants by name in 2005 — Austria schedules only the pure chemical DMT.

The official drug-checking and harm reduction service **checkit!** (operated by Suchthilfe Wien gGmbH, funded by the City of Vienna) states unambiguously [5]:

> **German:** "DMT-haltige Pflanzen sind nicht im SMG erfasst. Die Zucht DMT-haltiger Pflanzen zum Zweck der Herstellung von DMT ist aber strafbar."
>
> **English:** "Plants containing DMT are not covered by the SMG. However, the cultivation of plants containing DMT for the purpose of producing DMT is a criminal offence."

This statement is consistent with the text of § 27 SMG itself. The plant-specific criminal provisions in § 27 Abs 1 enumerate only [6]:

- **Z 2**: Opium poppy (Opiummohn), coca bush (Kokastrauch), and cannabis plants (Cannabispflanze) — criminalized when cultivated "zum Zweck der Suchtgiftgewinnung" (for the purpose of narcotic drug production)
- **Z 3**: Psilocin-, psilotin-, or psilocybin-containing mushrooms — criminalized when offered, provided, or cultivated "zum Zweck des Suchtgiftmissbrauchs" (for the purpose of narcotic drug misuse)

**DMT-containing plants do not appear in either Z 2 or Z 3.** This creates the legal grey area: MHRB (the dried plant bark) is not itself a scheduled substance, and possessing plant material is not enumerated as a criminal act in the plant-specific provisions. However, the general provision in § 27 Abs 1 Z 1 criminalizes the acquisition, possession, production, transport, import, export, offering, provision, or procurement of "Suchtgift" (narcotic drug) — and DMT is a narcotic drug [6]. The question is whether dried bark *containing* DMT constitutes "Suchtgift" in the legal sense.

**The checkit! position suggests it does not** — at least for the plant material itself. But checkit! is a harm reduction service, not a court. No Austrian court has ruled on whether MHRB constitutes "Suchtgift" under § 27 Abs 1 Z 1. The legal position of MHRB in Austria therefore remains untested.

| DMT (molecule) scheduled | Yes | Yes (Anlage I BtMG) | **Yes** (Suchtgiftverordnung) |

### The Grenzmenge (threshold quantity) for DMT

The Suchtgift-Grenzmengenverordnung (SGV — Narcotic Drugs Threshold Quantity Regulation) defines the Grenzmenge (threshold quantity) above which an offense escalates from § 27 SMG (misdemeanor, Vergehen) to § 28a SMG (felony, Verbrechen). The Grenzmenge for DMT is [7][8][9]:

**3.0 grams of DMT (pure active substance / Reinsubstanz)**

This is defined in Anlage (Annex) V of the SGV, which covers narcotics listed pursuant to the 1971 Convention on Psychotropic Substances [7]. The SGV specifies that the Grenzmenge refers to the pure substance content (Reinsubstanz), not the total weight of the preparation or plant material [7].

For comparison with other jurisdictions:
- **Germany**: 3.6g DMT (nicht geringe Menge, established by LG Frankenthal 2012 and LG Würzburg 2022 — via case law, not statute)
- **France**: No statutory quantity threshold

Austria's 3.0g threshold is **lower** than Germany's 3.6g, meaning less MHRB is needed to trigger the more serious offense category.

### What quantities of MHRB reach the Grenzmenge?

Using the DMT concentration ranges established by German forensic analyses (see [legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md](legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md)):

| MHRB quantity | At 1% DMT | At 2% DMT | At 3% DMT |
|---------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| 100g | 1.0g | 2.0g | **3.0g** — at Austrian threshold |
| 150g | 1.5g | **3.0g** — at threshold | 4.5g |
| 200g | 2.0g | 4.0g | 6.0g |
| 300g | **3.0g** — at threshold | 6.0g | 9.0g |
| 500g | 5.0g | 10.0g | 15.0g |

At the high end of forensic measurements (3% DMT, as found by the University of Saarland), **as little as 100g of MHRB could reach the Austrian Grenzmenge**. At the commonly assumed 1%, approximately 300g is required. The buyer cannot know the DMT concentration of their specific batch without laboratory analysis.

### The offense classification: § 27 vs. § 28a SMG

Like the French distinction between L3421-1 and 222-37 CP, the Austrian SMG creates a significant gap between the two tiers of offenses:

#### § 27 SMG — Unlawful handling of narcotics (below Grenzmenge)

§ 27 Abs 1 criminalizes the unauthorized acquisition, possession, production, transport, import, export, offering, provision, or procurement of narcotics [6]. The penalties are:

- **§ 27 Abs 1 (standard)**: Up to **1 year imprisonment** or fine of up to **360 Tagessätze** (daily rates)
- **§ 27 Abs 2 (personal use)**: If committed "ausschließlich zum persönlichen Gebrauch" (exclusively for personal use), reduced to up to **6 months imprisonment** or fine of up to 360 Tagessätze [6]

The personal use privilege is broadly interpreted: it extends to non-profit sharing among friends (e.g., passing a joint) [10].

#### § 28a SMG — Drug trafficking (at or above Grenzmenge)

§ 28a criminalizes the production, import, export, offering, provision, or procurement of narcotics **in quantities exceeding the Grenzmenge** [11]. The penalties escalate sharply:

- **§ 28a Abs 1 (standard)**: Up to **5 years imprisonment**
- **§ 28a Abs 2 (aggravated)**: 1 to 10 years (commercial activity + prior conviction, or as member of a criminal association)
- **§ 28a Abs 3 (mitigated for drug-dependent persons)**: Up to 3 years (for Abs 1 offenses) or up to 5 years (for Abs 2 offenses)
- **§ 28a Abs 4 (seriously aggravated)**: 1 to 15 years (criminal organization + prior conviction, or quantities exceeding 25x the Grenzmenge)
- **§ 28a Abs 5 (organized crime leadership)**: 10 to 20 years or life imprisonment

The gap between § 27 (max 6 months for personal use) and § 28a (up to 5 years) is enormous. For a cluster headache patient ordering MHRB, ensuring that any prosecution remains under § 27 is critical.

### "Helfen statt Strafen" — Austria's therapeutic approach

Austrian drug policy follows the principle of **"Helfen statt Strafen"** (help instead of punishment) [12][13]. This philosophy is embedded directly in the SMG's procedural provisions, making Austria's approach to personal-use drug offenses qualitatively different from both Germany and France.

When someone is detected with narcotics for personal use, the SMG mandates a health-oriented response:

1. A report is made to the **Gesundheitsbehörde** (health authority) [12]
2. The person is assessed by an **Amtsarzt** (public health officer) — in Vienna, by the Sucht- und Drogenkoordination Wien (SDW) [12]
3. The health authority can order measures including counseling, treatment, therapy, or monitoring [12]
4. The prosecutor **must** defer prosecution under § 35 SMG (see below)

### § 35 SMG — Mandatory prosecutorial diversion

This is the single most impactful provision in Austrian drug law for personal-use offenders. § 35 Abs 1 SMG provides for "vorläufiger Rücktritt von der Verfolgung" (provisional withdrawal from prosecution) [14]:

> "Die Staatsanwaltschaft hat unter den in den Abs. 3 bis 7 genannten Voraussetzungen [...] von der Verfolgung [...] vorläufig zurückzutreten."
>
> ("The prosecution **must** provisionally withdraw from prosecution under the conditions named in paragraphs 3 to 7.")

The verb "hat zurückzutreten" is **mandatory** ("must"), not discretionary ("kann" / "may"). The OGH (Oberster Gerichtshof — Austrian Supreme Court), in decision 11 Os 21/18z, confirmed that diversion under § 35 Abs 1 "is always required" when the statutory conditions are met [15].

**Key features of § 35 diversion:**

- **Applies to**: Offenses under § 27 Abs 1, § 27 Abs 2, or § 30 SMG committed exclusively for personal use [14]
- **Probationary period**: 1–2 years (typically 2 years) [14]
- **Conditions**: May include participation in health-related measures ordered by the Gesundheitsbehörde [14]
- **Outcome if successful**: Prosecution is **permanently withdrawn**. **No criminal record** (no entry in the Strafregister) [14]
- **Not limited to first offenses**: The last sentence of § 35 Abs 1 explicitly provides for diversion even during a probationary period from a prior diversion for further offenses of the same kind [14]
- **Does NOT apply above the Grenzmenge**: If the offense is classified under § 28a (quantities ≥ 3g DMT), § 35 Abs 1 does not apply. However, § 35 Abs 2 extends diversion to drug-dependent persons even for § 28/28a offenses [14]

**Comparison with other jurisdictions:**

| | France | Germany | **Austria** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Diversion mechanism | AFD (€200 fine, since 2020) | § 153a StPO (discretionary) | **§ 35 SMG (mandatory)** |
| Prosecutor discretion | Broad — can choose AFD, warning, prosecution | Full discretion | **No discretion** — must defer if conditions met |
| Criminal record | AFD: no; prosecution: yes | § 153a: no; conviction: yes | **Diversion: no criminal record** |
| Repeat offenders | AFD available for repeats | Less likely for repeats | **Explicitly available for repeat offenses** |

Austria's mandatory diversion is the strongest personal-use protection of the three jurisdictions.

## Netherlands Legal Status

### The purchase is legal in the Netherlands

MHRB (the raw plant bark) is legal to sell and purchase in the Netherlands [16]. Multiple Dutch smartshops openly sell it online as an ethnobotanical product. The plant itself and its unprocessed parts are not controlled under Dutch law [16].

### DMT is controlled

DMT is classified as a List I substance (hard drug) under the Dutch Opiumwet (Opium Act) [17]. The key distinction is the same as in Austria: the raw plant material is legal; the extracted alkaloid and preparations containing DMT are not.

### Cross-border implication

Purchasing MHRB from a Dutch smartshop is a legal transaction in the Netherlands. The question of criminal exposure under Austrian law begins when the package arrives in Austria — but given that MHRB is not explicitly scheduled under Austrian law, the legal basis for seizure is less clear-cut than in France (where the plant is scheduled by name) or even Germany (where courts are split but some prosecutors treat MHRB as a controlled substance). An Austrian customs officer encountering MHRB would need to establish that the plant material constitutes "Suchtgift" — which, per the checkit! position, it does not.

## How Investigations Are Initiated

### Vendor raids and customer list seizures

Based on documented patterns from Germany (see [legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md](legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md)), the primary enforcement mechanism for online MHRB purchases is the seizure of vendor customer lists when a seller is raided by police. An eBay shop based in Saarbrücken (Germany) that sold MHRB was raided, and customer data was seized. Buyers across Germany were subsequently contacted by investigators. Across multiple such shop raids, "thousands" reportedly received summons [18][19]. At least one Dutch online shop's customer data was also obtained by German authorities [20].

**No documented case of German or Dutch vendor customer list seizures reaching Austrian buyers has been identified**, despite extensive searching of German and Austrian forums (eve-rave.ch, Entheobotanik.net, DMT-Nexus, Reddit), attorney publications, ICEERS legal monitoring, and law enforcement press releases.

The mechanism for cross-border enforcement exists: Austrian authorities can issue a **European Investigation Order (EIO)** requesting Dutch or German authorities to share customer records. Conversely, German prosecutors who discover Austrian addresses in seized vendor customer lists could forward this information to Austrian counterparts. However, since MHRB is legal to sell in the Netherlands and its legal status in Austria is ambiguous, the basis for such a request is weaker than for clearly scheduled substances.

### Customs interception

Intra-EU parcels between Austria and the Netherlands are not subject to mandatory customs declarations under the EU single market [21]. However, Austrian customs (Zollamt Österreich, a branch of the Bundesministerium für Finanzen) conducts inspections at postal distribution centers using **drug-detection dogs** [22].

Documented examples from a February 2025 BMF press release [22]:

- **Vienna postal distribution center, December 2024**: 1,016g ketamine intercepted in a parcel, detected by service dog "Ike"
- **Vienna postal distribution center, January 2025**: 1+ kg herbal cannabis, detected by service dog "Ike"
- **Rankweil delivery facility, January 2025**: 3.1 kg cannabis in a parcel, detected by service dog "Joker"

These seizures demonstrate that Austrian customs actively screens postal parcels. However, the substances seized were cannabis and ketamine — both high-volume drugs with distinctive odors. Whether MHRB (an odorless brown plant powder) would trigger a drug dog's response is unclear.

### Scale of enforcement

The Bundeskriminalamt (BKA — Federal Criminal Intelligence Service) reported **37,310 SMG-related charges** in 2024, a 5.3% increase over 2023 [23]. The breakdown by substance [23]:

| Substance | Share of SMG reports (2024) |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| Cannabis | 54.9% |
| Cocaine | 17.6% |
| Heroin | 4.2% |
| Amphetamine | 4.2% |
| Methamphetamine | 3.2% |
| Ecstasy | 2.2% |
| Other | ~13.7% |

DMT is not tracked as a separate category. It would fall within "Other" alongside dozens of other rarely encountered substances. Psychedelics as a category are an enforcement afterthought — consistent with Tripsitter's assessment that "Austrian authorities have essentially deprioritized psychedelics" [24].

## What Happens After Detection

Whether triggered by customs interception, a vendor customer list, or a chance encounter, the Austrian procedure differs significantly from both Germany and France — primarily because of the mandatory diversion pathway.

### 1. Seizure (Beschlagnahme)

The substance is confiscated. If not yet identified, it may be sent for laboratory analysis by the Bundeskriminalamt or a regional Landeskriminalamt.

### 2. Investigation

A preliminary investigation (Ermittlungsverfahren) is opened under the authority of the Staatsanwaltschaft (public prosecution). The police may summon the suspect for questioning (Beschuldigtenvernehmung).

**Important**: As in Germany, defense attorneys advise exercising the right to silence and retaining counsel before making any statement. The right to silence (Aussageverweigerungsrecht) is guaranteed under § 164 Abs 1 StPO [25].

### 3. Health authority notification

For personal-use cases, the Gesundheitsbehörde (health authority) is notified. The suspect is assessed by an Amtsarzt (public health officer) to determine whether health-related measures (counseling, treatment, monitoring) are appropriate [12].

### 4. Prosecutorial decision — the § 35 pathway

For offenses classified under § 27 SMG (below the Grenzmenge), the prosecutor's decision is largely predetermined:

**If the offense is for personal use**, the prosecutor **must** defer prosecution under § 35 SMG [14][15]:

- Probationary period: 1–2 years
- Conditions may include participation in health-related measures
- If conditions are met: prosecution is permanently withdrawn, no criminal record
- If conditions are violated: prosecution resumes

**If § 35 does not apply** (e.g., evidence of supply, quantities above the Grenzmenge without drug dependency), the prosecutor may:

- Pursue diversion under general criminal law provisions (§ 198 StPO — Diversion)
- Issue an indictment (Anklageschrift) for prosecution before the Bezirksgericht (district court) for § 27 offenses, or the Landesgericht (regional court) for § 28a offenses

### Timeline

No published data exists on the typical duration of SMG proceedings involving MHRB or DMT. Based on the German experience with MHRB cases and general Austrian criminal procedure timelines, proceedings could range from a few months (if resolved via § 35 diversion) to over a year if prosecuted. The German precedent of 3-year delays between purchase and investigation should be assumed as a possibility.

## Court Cases and Outcomes

### No MHRB-specific Austrian cases

Despite extensive searching of Austrian legal databases (RIS), ICEERS legal monitoring, Erowid, German- and English-language forums (eve-rave.ch, Entheobotanik.net, DMT-Nexus, Reddit, Psychonaut.fr), attorney publications, and news archives, **no publicly documented Austrian prosecution specifically for ordering or possessing MHRB has been identified**.

### ICEERS ayahuasca arrest (~2020)

The only documented Austrian enforcement action involving a DMT-containing plant preparation is an ayahuasca-related case reported by ICEERS (International Center for Ethnobotanical Education, Research, and Service) [26]. A package was intercepted by Austrian police, and an individual was arrested on suspicion of international drug trafficking. ICEERS's Ayahuasca Defense Fund provided legal support. **No outcome details have been publicly reported** — neither conviction, acquittal, nor dismissal.

### ayahuasca.at — open operation without documented prosecution

The website ayahuasca.at openly advertises ayahuasca retreat experiences in the Austrian Alps [27]. Their legal defense rests on two claims:

1. That ayahuasca in ritual use is not considered a narcotic by the INCB (the UN body based in Vienna)
2. That the DMT experienced during retreats is endogenously produced by participants

No enforcement action against this operation has been publicly documented [27]. While the legal arguments presented on the website are questionable, the continued open operation suggests at minimum a de facto tolerance by Austrian authorities. This is consistent with the general enforcement deprioritization of psychedelics.

### German cases as comparators

Given the absence of Austrian case law, the German MHRB cases provide the closest legal comparators — particularly because the German and Austrian legal frameworks share the same structural feature: DMT (the molecule) is scheduled, but MHRB (the plant) is not explicitly named. The German acquittals (AG Rostock 2021, AG Geilenkirchen 2020, AG Freiburg 2024, AG Köln) and dismissals (Stern Strafrecht, HT Strafrecht) are described in detail in [legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md](legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md).

The key difference: in Germany, acquittals were won through courtroom argument (the "dyeing defense"). In Austria, the legal position may be more favorable from the outset — because the checkit! position and the statutory structure suggest MHRB is not covered by the SMG at all, making the prosecution's case harder to initiate.

## Available Defenses in Austria

### The structural defense: plants are not covered

Unlike in Germany, where the defense that plant materials are not criminal absent intoxication intent must be argued in court on a case-by-case basis, Austria's legal structure provides this defense at a more fundamental level. The statutory text of § 27 SMG enumerates specific plants (opium, coca, cannabis, psilocybin mushrooms) — and DMT-containing plants are not among them [6]. The checkit! service, an authoritative government-funded harm reduction body, has explicitly confirmed this reading [5].

A defense attorney could argue:

1. MHRB is not listed in the Suchtgiftverordnung
2. § 27 Abs 1 Z 2 and Z 3 enumerate specific plant categories — DMT plants are excluded
3. The official drug-checking service confirms plants containing DMT are not covered by the SMG
4. The prosecution must therefore prove that the dried bark constitutes "Suchtgift" under § 27 Abs 1 Z 1 — which requires demonstrating it is a preparation of DMT, not merely a plant that happens to contain it

**This defense has never been tested in an Austrian court.** Its viability is therefore uncertain. A prosecutor could counter-argue that dried MHRB — particularly when purchased from an online shop that markets it alongside other psychoactive products — constitutes a "Zubereitung" (preparation) of DMT and therefore falls under § 27 Abs 1 Z 1.

### Personal use classification → mandatory diversion

Even if the prosecution successfully argues that MHRB constitutes "Suchtgift," the defendant's strongest practical protection is ensuring the offense is classified as personal use under § 27 Abs 2. This triggers:

- Reduced maximum penalty (6 months instead of 1 year)
- **Mandatory** prosecutorial diversion under § 35 SMG
- No criminal record if probation is completed

Factors supporting personal use classification (analogous to Germany and France):

- Small quantity consistent with individual use
- No extraction equipment, scales, or packaging materials
- No communications suggesting sales or distribution
- Substance found at the defendant's home, not in transit to others
- No prior drug-related offenses

### The "dyeing defense" — structurally stronger in Austria

The German "dyeing defense" (Färbemittel-Verteidigung) has produced multiple acquittals in German courts (see [legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md](legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md)). In Austria, this defense is structurally stronger because the legal starting point is more favorable: if DMT-containing plants are "not covered by the SMG" per checkit!, then a person who purchases MHRB as a dye is not handling a controlled substance at all — the prosecution must affirmatively overcome this position rather than the defendant having to establish it.

However, MHRB's effectiveness as a dye has been scientifically tested by the Hamburg State Criminal Police Office (LKA Hamburg, published 2024): it produces only "rather pale brownish to beige" colors that fade after washing — not the vivid reds advertised by online sellers [28]. This finding could undermine the plausibility of a dyeing claim in any jurisdiction.

### Procedural defenses

A defense attorney (Strafverteidiger) may challenge:

- The legality of the parcel inspection (was there a valid legal basis?)
- Chain of custody for the seized substance
- Whether the defendant can be identified as the person who placed the order
- Whether the substance was actually analyzed and confirmed to contain DMT (in the AG Geilenkirchen German case, no analysis was performed at all)
- Compliance with procedural requirements during any questioning

### The cluster headache diagnosis

A cluster headache diagnosis is **not a legal defense** — no medical exemption for DMT or MHRB exists in Austrian law. However, a documented medical condition and evidence of self-treatment motivation may influence the health authority's (Gesundheitsbehörde) assessment and the conditions imposed during § 35 diversion. The "Helfen statt Strafen" philosophy is oriented toward treating drug use as a health issue, and a patient with a severe medical condition using a substance for symptom management fits this framework better than recreational use.

### The process is the punishment

Even with mandatory diversion, detection imposes burdens:

- Police contact, potentially including questioning
- Seizure and destruction of the substance
- Health authority assessment (Amtsarzt examination)
- 1–2 year probationary period under § 35
- Potential conditions (counseling, monitoring)
- Stress and uncertainty during the process
- If the Grenzmenge is exceeded: significantly more serious proceedings without the protection of mandatory § 35 Abs 1 diversion

## Sentencing: What Actually Happens

### The § 35 diversion pathway (most likely for personal use)

For a small quantity of MHRB classified as personal use, the most probable outcome is **mandatory diversion under § 35 SMG**:

- Probationary period: 1–2 years (typically 2 years)
- Possible conditions: counseling session with the Gesundheitsbehörde, health monitoring
- **No trial, no court appearance** (resolved at the prosecutorial level)
- **No criminal record** if probation is completed successfully
- Available even for repeat offenders

Historical data on the prevalence of diversion for drug offenses in Austria [29]:

- In 2000, **43.5% of criminal charges** under the misdemeanor provisions of the SMG were handled diversionarily by prosecutors under § 35
- In the 2023 Sicherheitsbericht, SMG diversion was the **second most frequent type of diversion** (10% of all diversions across all offense types)
- Regional prosecution discontinuation rates across all offense types (2023): Wien 62.4%, Innsbruck 61.6%, Graz 60.3%, Linz 57.1%. These are general rates, not drug-specific, but they illustrate the broad use of diversion and prosecutorial alternatives in the Austrian system

These figures cover all substances — overwhelmingly cannabis. For a rare substance like MHRB/DMT with no trafficking context and the additional complexity of the plant's ambiguous legal status, diversion or outright dismissal would be even more likely.

### If prosecuted under § 27 SMG (no diversion)

For cases that proceed to prosecution — typically because § 35 conditions are not met, the defendant refuses health measures, or the offense involves supply:

- **§ 27 Abs 2 (personal use)**: Max 6 months imprisonment or 360 Tagessätze
- **§ 27 Abs 1 (standard)**: Max 1 year imprisonment or 360 Tagessätze
- **§ 27 Abs 3 (commercial)**: Max 3 years

First offenses for personal use of non-mainstream substances rarely result in imprisonment. Common outcomes would be a fine (Geldstrafe) or suspended sentence (bedingte Freiheitsstrafe).

### If prosecuted under § 28a SMG (above Grenzmenge)

If the DMT quantity exceeds 3.0g (Grenzmenge), the offense escalates to § 28a:

- **§ 28a Abs 1**: Up to **5 years imprisonment**
- This is a Verbrechen (felony), not a Vergehen (misdemeanor) — with significantly more serious procedural consequences
- § 35 Abs 1 (mandatory diversion) does **not** apply
- § 35 Abs 2 can still apply if the person is drug-dependent
- The court can impose a fully suspended sentence (bedingte Strafnachsicht, § 43 StGB) for prison terms up to 2 years if a favorable prognosis exists. For terms between 2 and 3 years, partial suspension (§ 43a StGB) is possible — meaning at least one month and up to one-third of the sentence must be served, with the remainder suspended on probation

### Austrian drug offense statistics

The Bundeskriminalamt reported 37,310 SMG-related charges in 2024 [23]. Detailed conviction and sentencing statistics broken down by substance type are published in the annual Sicherheitsbericht and Gerichtliche Kriminalstatistik, but **no DMT-specific data exists** — the substance is too rare to appear as a separate category.

General SMG sentencing context [29][30]:

- **Diversion** (§ 35 SMG) handles a large share of personal-use cases — 43.5% historically for SMG misdemeanors
- The structural features of Austrian drug law — mandatory diversion under § 35 for personal use, and lower statutory maximum penalties (6 months under § 27 Abs 2 vs. up to 5 years under German § 29) — suggest fewer personal-use offenders face actual imprisonment than in Germany, though no direct statistical comparison exists
- The EUDA Drug Policy Profile for Austria describes the Austrian approach as featuring "the early creation of and lasting support for the principle of 'treatment instead of punishment'" for drug users [30]

### What this means for the cluster headache patient scenario

A person who orders a small quantity of MHRB (e.g., under 100g) for personal use, has no prior criminal record, and is detected would most likely face:

1. **Most probable**: Mandatory diversion under § 35 SMG — 1–2 year probation, health authority assessment, no criminal record. Or outright dismissal if the prosecution accepts the checkit! position that plants are not covered.
2. **If not diverted**: A fine or suspended sentence under § 27, with actual imprisonment extremely unlikely for a first offense
3. **Worst case**: If classified under § 28a (>3g DMT) and personal use is not accepted — up to 5 years imprisonment, though this scenario requires both a large quantity and failure of the personal-use argument

| **Criminal exposure if caught** | Range from mandatory diversion with no criminal record (best realistic case) to theoretically 5 years under § 28a (worst case, extremely unlikely for personal use). |
| **"Dyeing" defense** | Structurally stronger than in Germany — the plant itself is arguably not covered by the SMG, so the prosecution must prove it constitutes "Suchtgift." But untested in Austrian courts. |
| **No prior drug record** | Favors § 35 diversion. Not strictly required (§ 35 applies even to repeat offenders), but strengthens the position. |
| **Cluster headache diagnosis** | Not legally relevant as a defense. May positively influence the health authority's assessment during § 35 diversion. |
| **Repeat orders** | Each additional order increases cumulative detection risk. Unlike in Germany, it does not prevent § 35 diversion (which applies even to repeat offenders). |
| **Digital footprint** | If investigated, DMT-related content found on devices (extraction guides, forum posts, this guide) could support the argument that MHRB was intended for DMT extraction/use, undermining the "plant not covered by SMG" defense. |
| **Time delay** | German precedent shows delays of up to 3 years between purchase and investigation. Similar delays should be assumed for Austria. |

## Three-Country Comparison

| Aspect | France | Germany | **Austria** |
|--------|--------|---------|-------------|
| **MHRB legal status** | Explicitly scheduled by name (2005) | Not scheduled; criminal only via DMT content | **Not scheduled; checkit!: "not covered by the SMG"** |
| **Intent defense (dyeing)** | Not available | Multiple acquittals on dyeing grounds | **Structurally built in** — plant not enumerated in § 27 |
| **Quantity thresholds** | None statutory | 3.6g DMT (nicht geringe Menge) | **3.0g DMT (Grenzmenge)** |
| **Personal use: max penalty** | 1 year + €3,750 (L3421-1) | Fine or up to 5 years (§ 29) | **6 months or 360 Tagessätze (§ 27 Abs 2)** |
| **Supply/trafficking: max penalty** | 10 years + €7.5M (222-37) | 1–15 years (§ 29a/§ 30) | **5 years (§ 28a Abs 1)** |
| **Diversion for personal use** | AFD (€200 fine, discretionary) | § 153a StPO (discretionary) | **§ 35 SMG (mandatory)** |
| **Criminal record from diversion** | AFD: no standard record | § 153a: no | **§ 35: no** |
| **Documented MHRB cases** | None found | Multiple (acquittals, dismissals, convictions) | **None found** |
| **Most likely outcome (small quantity, first offense)** | €200 AFD or dismissal | Dismissal (§ 153/153a/170(2)) or fine | **Mandatory diversion (§ 35), no criminal record** |

**Summary**: Austria's law is the most favorable of the three jurisdictions for a personal-use MHRB case. The plant is not explicitly scheduled, the checkit! position is that DMT-containing plants are not covered by the SMG, personal-use diversion is mandatory (not discretionary), and no MHRB prosecution has been documented. The main risk factors are: (1) the legal position has never been tested in court, (2) the Grenzmenge (3.0g DMT) is lower than Germany's threshold, and (3) the cross-border enforcement mechanisms (vendor customer list seizures) that have affected German buyers could theoretically reach Austrian buyers.

## Evidence Quality Note

This analysis draws on Austrian legislation (RIS — Rechtsinformationssystem des Bundes), the official drug-checking service checkit! Wien, ICEERS legal monitoring, Erowid legal archives, the Austrian BKA Suchtmittelbericht, Austrian defense attorney publications, the EUDA Drug Policy Profile for Austria, and German-language forum discussions. Key limitations:

- **No documented MHRB-specific prosecution exists for Austria.** The ICEERS ayahuasca arrest (~2020) is the closest case, and no outcome has been published. No Austrian court has issued a decision specifically addressing MHRB possession or the question of whether dried bark constitutes "Suchtgift."
- **The checkit! position — that DMT-containing plants are not covered by the SMG — is authoritative but not binding.** checkit! is a government-funded harm reduction service, not a court or a prosecutorial authority. Its interpretation is consistent with the statutory text but has never been tested in litigation. A prosecutor could take a different view.
- **No DMT-specific enforcement statistics exist.** DMT is not tracked as a separate substance category by the BKA, the Ministry of Justice, or Statistik Austria. All enforcement and sentencing data presented covers all drugs combined — dominated by cannabis.
- **The § 35 diversion statistics** (43.5% of SMG misdemeanor charges handled diversionarily) cover all substances, not MHRB or DMT specifically. Whether § 35 has ever been applied to a DMT case is unknown.
- **Austrian sentencing statistics** for SMG offenses broken down by sentence type (fine vs. suspended imprisonment vs. actual imprisonment) could not be extracted from the available sources. The Sicherheitsbericht and Gerichtliche Kriminalstatistik contain this data but were not available in parseable form.
- **Forum reports** (eve-rave.ch) are anecdotal, often do not specify the country (Austria vs. Germany vs. Switzerland), and may not represent typical enforcement outcomes.
- **Cross-border enforcement patterns** (vendor customer list seizures reaching Austrian authorities) are extrapolated from documented German cases; no equivalent Austrian case has been documented.
- **The ayahuasca.at operation** continues to advertise openly. Whether this reflects legal tolerance, enforcement oversight, or strategic non-prosecution is unknown.
- **The Grenzmenge of 3.0g DMT** was confirmed from the SGV text, but no Austrian court decision applying this threshold to DMT specifically has been identified.
