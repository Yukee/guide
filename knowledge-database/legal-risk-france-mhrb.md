# Legal Risk: Ordering Mimosa Hostilis Root Bark to France

This entry analyzes the legal risk of a specific scenario: a French cluster headache patient orders Mimosa hostilis root bark (MHRB) from a Netherlands-based online shop. Unlike Germany, where MHRB exists in a legal gray area because only DMT (not the plant) is scheduled, **France explicitly banned Mimosa hostilis by name in 2005**. There is no "dyeing defense," no intent-based loophole, and no ambiguity: possessing MHRB is a criminal offense regardless of stated purpose. The practical question is not *whether* it is illegal, but *how severely* the offense will be treated — and here, French law offers a wide range of outcomes, from a €200 fixed fine to a theoretical maximum of 10 years' imprisonment, depending primarily on whether the offense is classified as personal use or acquisition/détention for supply.

For the international and US-focused legal context, see [legal-status-and-detection.md](legal-status-and-detection.md). For the German-specific analysis, see [legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md](legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md). For the Austrian-specific analysis, see [legal-risk-austria-mhrb.md](legal-risk-austria-mhrb.md). For the UK-specific analysis, see [legal-risk-uk-mhrb.md](legal-risk-uk-mhrb.md).

## French Legal Framework

### DMT under French law

DMT (dimethyltryptamine) is classified as a stupéfiant (narcotic) under French law, listed in the arrêté du 22 février 1990 fixing the list of substances classified as narcotics [1]. This arrêté implements France's obligations under the 1971 UN Convention on Psychotropic Substances. DMT appears alongside other tryptamines (DET, DPT, psilocybin, psilocin) and classic hallucinogens (LSD, mescaline) [1].

The criminal provisions governing narcotics offenses are split between two codes:

- **Code de la santé publique (CSP)**: Article L3421-1 criminalizes the *use* of narcotics [2]
- **Code pénal (CP)**: Articles 222-34 through 222-43 criminalize trafficking, transport, détention, acquisition, offre, cession, and other handling of narcotics [3]

### MHRB is explicitly scheduled — the Arrêté du 20 avril 2005

This is the single most important legal fact for this analysis. On May 3, 2005, the Journal Officiel published the arrêté du 20 avril 2005, which amended the 1990 scheduling decree to explicitly add the following plants and alkaloids to the list of controlled substances [4]:

**Plants added:**
- Banisteriopsis caapi
- **Mimosa hostilis** (listed by this name)
- Psychotria viridis
- Diplopterys cabrerana
- Banisteriopsis rusbyana
- Peganum harmala

**Alkaloids added:**
- Harmine, harmaline, tétrahydroharmine (THH), harmol, harmalol

France is the **only country in the world** that has explicitly scheduled these ayahuasca-related plants by name [5][6]. Most other countries — including Germany, the US, the UK, and Canada — schedule only DMT (the molecule), leaving the legal status of DMT-containing plants in varying degrees of ambiguity.

**The consequence is absolute**: in France, possessing Mimosa hostilis root bark is possessing a scheduled substance. The intent of the possessor — whether for dyeing, botanical study, or DMT extraction — is irrelevant to the classification of the offense. There is no "dyeing defense" as exists in German jurisprudence (see [legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md](legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md)).

#### Why France scheduled the plants: the 100 days of ayahuasca

The 2005 decree has an unusual legislative history. On **January 12, 2005**, the Cour d'appel de Paris (10ème chambre) acquitted six members of the Santo Daime church — five French and one Brazilian national — who had been charged with drug trafficking for importing and using ayahuasca tea [7][8]. The court's reasoning was straightforward: the 1990 scheduling decree listed DMT (the molecule) but not the plants from which it is derived. Ayahuasca tea, being a plant preparation rather than purified DMT, was therefore not a scheduled substance. The seized tea was ordered returned to the defendants [7].

This acquittal alarmed French health authorities. Within **four months**, the government enacted the arrêté du 20 avril 2005, adding the ayahuasca plants themselves — including Mimosa hostilis — to the controlled substances list [4]. The academic literature describes this as "one hundred days of ayahuasca in France" — the brief window between the court victory and the legislative closure [8].

The decree effectively overturned the Court of Appeals' reasoning for all future cases. Since May 3, 2005, there has been no viable legal argument that MHRB is not a controlled substance in France.

### No statutory quantity thresholds

Unlike Germany, where the "nicht geringe Menge" (not small amount) threshold of 3.6g DMT triggers escalated charges under § 29a BtMG, **French law establishes no statutory quantity thresholds** for narcotics offenses [9][10]. There is no defined boundary between "small" and "large" amounts that automatically changes the applicable criminal provision.

Quantity matters in practice — it influences how prosecutors classify the offense and what sentence courts impose — but it does so through prosecutorial and judicial discretion, not through statutory triggers. A person caught with 50g of MHRB and a person caught with 5 kg face the same criminal provisions; the difference lies in how the procureur (prosecutor) chooses to handle the case.

### The offense classification battleground: usage simple vs. acquisition/détention

The most consequential legal question for someone caught with MHRB in France is **which criminal provision applies**. The gap between the two possibilities is enormous.

#### Usage simple — Article L3421-1 CSP

Article L3421-1 of the Code de la santé publique criminalizes the illicit use ("usage illicite") of any substance or plant classified as a narcotic [2]. The maximum penalty is:

- **1 year imprisonment** and **€3,750 fine**

Aggravated penalties of **5 years imprisonment and €75,000 fine** apply when usage is committed by persons exercising public authority or by transport personnel in safety-critical roles [2]. Complementary penalties may include: mandatory drug awareness course (stage de sensibilisation), suspension of driving license, confiscation of the substance, and prohibition from certain professional activities [2].

Since September 2020, usage simple can also be resolved via the **amende forfaitaire délictuelle (AFD)** — a €200 fixed fine (see "What Happens After Detection" below) [11].

#### Acquisition, détention, transport — Article 222-37 Code pénal

Article 222-37 of the Code pénal criminalizes the "transport, détention, offre, cession, acquisition, or emploi illicite" of narcotics [3]. The maximum penalty is:

- **10 years imprisonment** and **€7,500,000 fine**

This is the same article that applies to drug dealers. Ordering MHRB online technically involves "acquisition" (purchasing) and potentially "importation" — both of which fall under Article 222-37 or related provisions.

#### Which provision applies? The Cour de Cassation's resolution

The overlap between L3421-1 (usage) and 222-37 (acquisition/détention) creates a critical ambiguity: someone who buys drugs for personal use has committed both "usage" (a 1-year maximum offense) and "acquisition" (a 10-year maximum offense). French courts have addressed this through the principle of **spécialité**: the Cour de Cassation has ruled that **Article L3421-1 takes precedence** and excludes application of Article 222-37 when the narcotics were exclusively intended for the defendant's personal consumption (see notably Crim. 14 mars 2017, n° 16-81.805; confirmed by Crim. 23 janvier 2019, n° 18-82.506 and Crim. 11 mai 2021, n° 20-84.412) [12][13].

In practice, this means:

- **If the MHRB was for personal use**: The offense should be classified under L3421-1 (max 1 year), not 222-37 (max 10 years)
- **If there is evidence of supply, sharing, or distribution**: Article 222-37 applies with its drastically higher penalties
- **The burden of classification falls on the prosecution**: Prosecutors decide which provision to charge. Indicators they consider include: quantity, packaging, financial records, communications suggesting sales, presence of scales or distribution equipment, and whether the quantity is consistent with personal use

**For a cluster headache patient ordering a small quantity for self-treatment, L3421-1 should apply** — but the patient has no guarantee of this classification at the moment of interception.

## Netherlands Legal Status

### The purchase is legal in the Netherlands

MHRB (the raw plant bark) is legal to sell and purchase in the Netherlands [14]. Multiple Dutch smartshops openly sell it online as an ethnobotanical product. The plant itself and its unprocessed parts are not controlled under Dutch law [14].

### DMT is controlled

DMT is classified as a List I substance (hard drug) under the Dutch Opiumwet (Opium Act) [15]. The key distinction is the same as in most countries other than France: the raw plant material is legal; the extracted alkaloid and preparations containing DMT are not.

### Cross-border implication

Purchasing MHRB from a Dutch smartshop is a legal transaction in the Netherlands. The criminal exposure under French law begins when the package crosses into French territory or arrives at the recipient's address in France. Unlike the German situation — where the legal status of the plant material itself is debatable — a French customs officer encountering MHRB has an unambiguous basis for seizure: the plant is scheduled by name.

## How Investigations Are Initiated

### Vendor raids and customer list seizures

Based on documented patterns from Germany (see [legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md](legal-risk-germany-mhrb.md)), the primary enforcement mechanism for online MHRB purchases is not customs screening individual parcels — it is the seizure of vendor customer lists when a seller is raided by police.

**No MHRB-specific vendor raids affecting French customers have been publicly documented**, despite extensive searching of French and English-language forums (Psychoactif.org, Psychonaut.fr, DMT-Nexus, Reddit), attorney publications, court databases, and law enforcement press releases. This absence is notable but does not mean the risk is zero.

The mechanism for cross-border enforcement exists: French authorities can issue a **European Investigation Order (EIO)** requesting Dutch authorities to seize customer records from a Dutch vendor. However, since MHRB is legal to sell in the Netherlands, Dutch authorities have no independent reason to raid a vendor — a French request would need to initiate the process. This is resource-intensive for what French prosecutors would likely treat as a low-priority offense.

The German experience demonstrates that the mechanism works: customer lists from a German-based eBay MHRB seller and at least one Dutch online shop were seized and used to identify buyers across Germany, with investigation delays of up to 3 years [16]. If a Dutch MHRB vendor's customer data reaches French authorities through a similar operation, French buyers would face investigation.

### Customs interception

Intra-EU parcels between France and the Netherlands are not subject to mandatory customs declarations under the EU single market [17]. However, French customs (Direction générale des douanes et droits indirects — DGDDI) conduct random and targeted inspections at postal sorting centers [18].

The Wissous postal sorting center in Île-de-France — the largest in the Paris region — employs approximately 30 customs agents who can sample and test suspicious packages [18]. Colorimetric field tests can be performed on site; suspicious substances may be sent for laboratory analysis [19]. Since the COVID-19 pandemic, seizure rates at postal centers have increased faster than parcel volume [18].

The fact that MHRB is explicitly scheduled in France simplifies the customs response: an agent who identifies the substance needs only to confirm it is Mimosa hostilis — there is no need for the intent-based analysis that German customs would face. However, there is no publicly available data on interception rates for intra-EU parcels, and MHRB (a brown plant powder) is not distinctive enough to reliably trigger suspicion without further information.

### Scale of enforcement

No MHRB-specific enforcement statistics exist for France. DMT is not tracked as a separate substance category by the OFDT (Observatoire français des drogues et des tendances addictives) or the Ministry of Justice [9].

To put the enforcement priority in context: in 2023, French police recorded approximately 267,000 drug use offenses, of which the vast majority involved cannabis [9]. Even LSD and psilocybin — better-known psychedelics — represent a negligible fraction of French drug enforcement. DMT/MHRB would be a fraction of that fraction. The 2025 narcotrafic law focused entirely on organized trafficking networks and did not alter the legal status or enforcement priority of psychedelics [20].

## What Happens After Detection

Whether triggered by customs interception or a vendor customer list, the French criminal procedure sequence differs from the German one.

### 1. Seizure (saisie)

The package is confiscated. If the substance has not yet been identified, it may be sent for laboratory analysis.

### 2. Investigation (enquête)

A preliminary investigation (enquête préliminaire) or flagrancy investigation (enquête de flagrance) is opened. The investigation is conducted under the authority of the procureur de la République (public prosecutor).

### 3. Possible police custody (garde à vue)

The suspect may be placed in garde à vue (police custody) for up to **48 hours** for drug offenses [21]. During garde à vue, the suspect has the right to a lawyer from the first hour, the right to silence, and the right to a medical examination. This is a significantly more invasive first contact than the German system, where suspects typically receive a written summons (Vorladung).

### 4. Prosecutorial decision

The procureur de la République decides how to handle the case. The options, from lightest to heaviest:

**Classement sans suite (dismissal)**: The case is dropped for insufficient evidence or lack of public interest [9]. No sanction, no record.

**Avertissement pénal probatoire (probationary criminal warning)**: Introduced by the loi n° 2021-1729 du 22 décembre 2021 (entered into force January 1, 2023), this replaced the former "rappel à la loi" [22]. The suspect is formally warned, with conditions that may include attending a drug awareness course. If conditions are met, the case is closed.

**Amende forfaitaire délictuelle (AFD — fixed criminal fine)**: Since September 2020, police officers or gendarmes can impose an immediate €200 fine for usage simple of narcotics, without the case going to a prosecutor or court [11][23]. Key details:

- **€150** if paid within 15 days (reduced amount)
- **€200** standard amount
- **€450** if paid after 45 days (increased amount)
- No criminal trial, no court appearance
- **Does not result in a criminal conviction** in the traditional sense — it is recorded in the TAJ (Traitement des Antécédents Judiciaires, the police records database) but does not appear on the standard casier judiciaire (criminal record) bulletin n°3
- **Only applies to usage simple** (L3421-1). If the offense is classified as acquisition/détention (222-37 CP), the AFD cannot be used
- As of 2024, approximately **200,000 AFDs** were issued annually for drug use, representing roughly **76% of all drug use cases** processed by law enforcement [11][23]
- **Upcoming change**: In December 2024, President Macron announced the AFD for drug use will increase to **€500** (from €200) [11]

**Composition pénale (criminal composition)**: A negotiated resolution proposed by the prosecutor, which may include a fine, community service, drug awareness course, or other conditions. If accepted by the defendant and validated by a judge, it concludes the case without trial [9].

**Poursuites (prosecution)**: The case is sent to the tribunal correctionnel for trial. This is the most burdensome path and is more likely for larger quantities, repeat offenders, or cases classified under 222-37 CP.

### Timeline

No published data exists on the typical duration of MHRB-specific cases in France. Based on general French criminal procedure timelines and the German experience with MHRB cases, proceedings could take anywhere from an immediate AFD (resolved on the spot) to months or years if a vendor customer list triggers a retroactive investigation.

## Court Cases and Outcomes

### The Santo Daime cases

The most significant French jurisprudence on DMT-containing plant materials involves ayahuasca, not MHRB. These cases are relevant because the same arrêté du 20 avril 2005 that schedules ayahuasca plants also schedules Mimosa hostilis.

#### Acquittal: Cour d'appel de Paris (January 12, 2005)

The Cour d'appel de Paris, 10ème chambre, acquitted six members of the Santo Daime church — five French and one Brazilian — who had been charged with drug trafficking and organized crime for importing and using ayahuasca tea [7][8]. The court found that ayahuasca (a plant preparation) was not itself a scheduled substance, since only DMT (the molecule) was listed in the 1990 decree. The seized ayahuasca was ordered returned to the defendants [7].

This acquittal was legislatively overturned within four months by the arrêté du 20 avril 2005 [4].

#### Conviction and ongoing proceedings: Claude Bauchet

Claude Bauchet, a prominent Santo Daime leader in France, has faced multiple prosecutions spanning two decades [5][24]:

- **2004**: Convicted along with six other Santo Daime members. 18 months suspended imprisonment for import, possession, and use of narcotics
- **January 2005**: Acquitted by the Cour d'appel de Paris (the ruling described above)
- **Post-2005**: With the new decree in place, the prior legal defense was no longer available
- **2019**: Arrested again under the 2005 classification
- **December 2024**: The Conseil d'État (Council of State) rejected Bauchet's challenge to the constitutionality of the 2005 decree. Bauchet and a co-defendant were sentenced to **10 months suspended imprisonment** [24]

Bauchet's 2024 defense argued that a January 2022 regulatory update redefined narcotics as substances that must be both "toxic" and "addictive," and that ayahuasca meets neither criterion based on current scientific evidence. The Conseil d'État rejected this constitutional challenge [24].

### Conviction: Tribunal correctionnel de Vesoul (May 3, 2018)

Outside the Santo Daime context, the only documented French conviction for importing a DMT-containing plant preparation involves a Peruvian shaman and a French associate [26][27]. On October 6, 2016, customs officers intercepted the pair at Saint-Sauveur (Haute-Saône) with approximately six kilograms of ayahuasca. The shaman — who claimed 36 years of experience with the substance — was traveling to the Vosges region to lead a three-day ceremonial seminar with 26 participants. His French associate had raised funds to finance the shaman's travel.

The Tribunal correctionnel de Vesoul convicted both defendants on May 3, 2018. Sentence:

- **12 months imprisonment** (2 months firm — already served during pre-trial detention — 10 months suspended)
- **5-year ban from French territory** for the shaman
- Defense counsel announced plans to appeal

This case is relevant because it demonstrates that French courts apply the post-2005 framework to convict for importing DMT-containing plant preparations outside the Santo Daime religious context. The sentence — 2 months firm for 6 kg of ayahuasca in a commercial ceremony setting — provides a concrete data point for sentencing severity. It also illustrates the "process is the punishment" dynamic: the shaman spent two months in pre-trial detention before trial.

However, this case differs significantly from the cluster headache patient scenario: it involved a large quantity (6 kg), a commercial ceremony operation, physical importation (not online ordering), and a foreign national — all aggravating factors that would not apply to a patient ordering a small quantity of MHRB for personal use.

### No documented MHRB-specific prosecutions

Despite extensive searching of French forums (Psychoactif.org, Psychonaut.fr), attorney publications, court databases, ICEERS legal monitoring, and news archives, **no publicly documented French prosecution specifically for ordering or possessing MHRB has been identified**.

This absence likely reflects a combination of:

1. **Rarity**: MHRB use in France is rare compared to cannabis, cocaine, or even other psychedelics
2. **Disposition without published decisions**: Cases resolved via AFD, classement sans suite, or composition pénale do not generate published court decisions
3. **Lack of vendor-triggered mass investigations**: Unlike Germany, where a single vendor raid generated hundreds of customer investigations, no equivalent French event has been documented

The absence of published cases does **not** indicate that enforcement does not occur — only that it has not generated publicly accessible legal precedents.

### Forum reports

Discussions on Psychoactif.org include a thread titled "Douane et Mimosa Hostilis" where users discuss customs risks associated with ordering MHRB to France [19]. Participants note that MHRB is "theoretically forbidden for sale in France" and discuss pricing (€60–115/kg from various suppliers). No participant reports a completed prosecution, but the discussion reflects awareness of the legal risk. A separate thread describes a customs incident where a parcel from a research chemical supplier was opened by customs in Bordeaux, with a 10% sample taken for colorimetric testing [19][25].

## Available Defenses in France

### The "dyeing defense" does not apply

In Germany, multiple courts have acquitted MHRB defendants who claimed they purchased the material for textile dyeing, on the basis that plants containing controlled substances are only criminal to possess if intended for intoxication. **This defense is legally irrelevant in France.** The arrêté du 20 avril 2005 schedules Mimosa hostilis as a controlled substance in its own right — not because it contains DMT, but by name [4]. The possessor's intent does not change the classification of the substance.

A French defendant who argues "I bought it for dyeing" would face the response: "It is nonetheless a scheduled substance, and you possessed it without authorization."

### What defenses ARE available

#### Personal use classification

The most impactful defense strategy is ensuring the offense is classified as **usage simple (L3421-1 CSP)** rather than **acquisition/détention (222-37 CP)**. The difference — 1 year maximum vs. 10 years maximum — is the single largest variable in the defendant's legal exposure.

Factors supporting personal use classification:
- Small quantity consistent with individual use
- No extraction equipment, scales, or packaging materials
- No communications suggesting sales or distribution
- No financial records showing drug-related transactions
- Substance found at the defendant's home, not in transit to others
- No prior drug-related offenses

#### First offense with no prior record

A first-time offender with no criminal history is significantly more likely to receive the lightest available disposition: an AFD (€200), an avertissement pénal probatoire (warning), or a classement sans suite (dismissal). French prosecutors exercise broad discretion, and a clean record is the single strongest mitigating factor [9].

#### Procedural defenses

A defense attorney (avocat pénaliste) may challenge:
- The legality of the package inspection (was there a valid legal basis for the search?)
- Chain of custody for the seized substance
- Whether the defendant can be identified as the person who placed the order (as in Germany, the name on a package does not definitively prove the recipient ordered it)
- Garde à vue procedural requirements (access to lawyer, right to silence, medical examination)

#### The cluster headache diagnosis

A cluster headache diagnosis is **not a legal defense** — no medical exemption for DMT or MHRB exists in French law. However, a documented medical condition and evidence of self-treatment motivation may influence prosecutorial discretion and sentencing. French law does not provide a formal mechanism for this, but prosecutors and judges exercise broad discretion.

### The process is the punishment

Even defendants whose cases end with a €200 AFD or dismissal endure significant burden:
- Possible garde à vue (up to 48 hours in police custody)
- Seizure and potential destruction of the substance
- Potential house search if larger quantities are suspected
- Attorney fees (though these may be reimbursed if charges are dropped)
- Recording in the TAJ (police antecedents database), which persists even after a case is dismissed
- Stress and uncertainty during proceedings

If the case is **not** resolved via AFD — for example, because the quantity is large or the offense is classified under 222-37 CP — the process becomes significantly more burdensome, potentially involving months of proceedings before the tribunal correctionnel.

## Sentencing: What Actually Happens

### The AFD pathway (most likely for small personal quantities)

For a small quantity of MHRB classified as usage simple, the most probable outcome is an **amende forfaitaire délictuelle**:

- **€200 fixed fine** (€150 if paid within 15 days; €450 if late)
- No court appearance, no trial
- Not recorded on the casier judiciaire (criminal record) in the standard sense
- Resolved at the point of contact with law enforcement

As of 2024, the AFD handles approximately 76% of all drug use cases in France [11][23]. The AFD amount is set to increase to €500 following a December 2024 announcement by President Macron. The remainder are handled through other prosecutorial alternatives (warning, composition pénale) or prosecution.

### If prosecuted for usage simple (L3421-1 CSP)

For cases that proceed to prosecution under L3421-1 — typically repeat offenders, aggravating circumstances, or cases where the AFD was not applied:

- Maximum: **1 year imprisonment + €3,750 fine**
- First offenses for personal use of non-mainstream substances rarely result in imprisonment
- Common outcomes: suspended sentence (sursis), fine, mandatory drug awareness course (stage de sensibilisation aux dangers de l'usage de produits stupéfiants)
- Injonction thérapeutique (therapeutic injunction): the defendant agrees to enter treatment; if treatment is completed, charges may be dropped [9]

### If prosecuted under Article 222-37 CP (acquisition/détention)

If the offense is classified under 222-37 — because the quantity is large, there is evidence of distribution, or the prosecution does not accept the personal use argument:

- Maximum: **10 years imprisonment + €7,500,000 fine**
- However, the Cour de Cassation's jurisprudence should redirect personal-use cases to L3421-1
- An experienced defense attorney would raise this as a primary argument
- If the 222-37 classification stands, sentencing depends heavily on quantity and circumstances

### General French drug sentencing context

The OFDT's 2024 legal framework workbook provides the most authoritative overview of French drug offense processing [9]:

**Case disposition for drug offenses (all substances, 2022–2023):**

| Disposition type | Approximate share |
|-----------------|------------------|
| Amende forfaitaire délictuelle (AFD) | ~76% of drug use cases (2024) |
| Alternative measures (warning, awareness course, composition pénale) | ~20–30% |
| Prosecution before tribunal correctionnel | ~15–25% |
| Classement sans suite (dismissal) | ~10–15% |

These figures cover all substances — overwhelmingly cannabis. For a rare substance like MHRB/DMT with no trafficking context, the proportion handled via AFD, alternative measures, or dismissal would likely be even higher.

**Drug offense breakdown by type (2023) [9]:**

| Offense type | Share |
|-------------|-------|
| Usage (personal consumption) | 37% |
| Détention-acquisition | 57% |
| Offre/cession (distribution) | 5% |
| Commerce-transport | 1% |
| Trafic (trafficking) | 0.09% |

The fact that détention-acquisition (57%) vastly exceeds usage (37%) in recorded offenses illustrates that French police often classify possession under the more serious heading — making the L3421-1 vs. 222-37 distinction practically important, not just theoretical.

### What this means for the cluster headache patient scenario

A person who orders a small quantity of MHRB (e.g., under 100g) for personal use, has no prior criminal record, and is detected would most likely face:

1. **Most probable**: €200 AFD or case dismissal — especially if the quantity is small and there is no evidence of distribution
2. **If not resolved via AFD**: Warning (avertissement pénal probatoire), awareness course, or composition pénale with a moderate fine
3. **If prosecuted**: A fine or suspended sentence under L3421-1, with actual imprisonment extremely unlikely for a first offense
4. **Worst case**: If classified under 222-37 CP and the personal use argument fails — significant custodial exposure, though still mitigated by first-offense status and small quantity

| Aspect | France | Germany |

The paradox: France's law is *stricter on paper* (no gray area, plant explicitly banned) but potentially *comparable or milder in practice* for small personal-use quantities, because the AFD system provides a rapid, low-severity resolution that Germany lacks.

## Evidence Quality Note

This analysis draws on French legislation (Légifrance), OFDT reports, ICEERS legal monitoring, court decisions (Cour d'appel de Paris, Conseil d'État), Erowid legal archives, and French-language forum discussions. Key limitations:

- **No documented MHRB-specific prosecutions exist for France.** The Santo Daime/ayahuasca cases are the closest jurisprudence, but they involve a different preparation. No French court has published a decision specifically addressing Mimosa hostilis possession.
- **No MHRB-specific enforcement statistics exist.** DMT is not tracked as a separate substance category by the OFDT, the Ministry of Justice, or the Ministry of the Interior. All sentencing and disposition data presented covers all drugs combined — dominated by cannabis.
- **The AFD statistics (200,000/year, ~50% of drug use cases) cover all substances**, not MHRB or DMT specifically. Whether the AFD is applied to MHRB cases specifically is unknown.
- **Forum reports** (Psychoactif.org, Psychonaut.fr) are anecdotal and may not represent typical enforcement outcomes.
- **The Cour de Cassation jurisprudence** on L3421-1 vs. 222-37 applies to narcotics generally; no case specifically addressing this distinction for MHRB has been identified.
- **Attorney analyses for MHRB/DMT in France** are far less numerous than in Germany, where the legal gray area has generated extensive published commentary. French defense attorneys have not published the kind of MHRB-specific practice guides that German attorneys have.
- **The April 2005 decree's constitutionality was challenged** by Claude Bauchet in 2024 and **rejected** by the Conseil d'État. This forecloses the most obvious constitutional avenue for challenging MHRB's scheduling.
- **Cross-border enforcement patterns** (vendor customer list seizures reaching French authorities) are extrapolated from documented German cases; no equivalent French case has been publicly documented.
